September 10, 2023

46. Adelaide

by Joanne Amarisa
Adelaide, Australia. Sunday, 3rd September 2023.

My Dear Friend,

About 22 hours ago, I was sitting on a hillside overlooking the River Torrens, a stream that stretches across the west to east of Adelaide's CBD. My friend and I had spent the weekend together on an agreed-upon "introvert vacation"—we whisked ourselves momentarily away from the busyness of our lives to spend the majority of a trip sitting on grass, going on long strolls, and getting back early to our hotel room to enjoy the bliss of showered-and-in-bed-by-8PM.

On the riverside, we sat in silence and people-watched as the sun sank lower. To my right, two women sat next to each other, and one of them pulled out a ukulele and began to sing. To the further right, a man was sitting by his bike, staring at the surroundings much like I was. I wondered about his thoughts and his life. What brings us to spend a Saturday evening by the riverside?

Day 1
 
We landed close to Friday noon and wheeled our luggage to our hotel on King William St. Adelaide fit the "template starter pack" of any metro city I've visited in Australia—a State Library; an Art Gallery; a Big Park Named After a Governor; a cathedral called St. Peter's; a Royal Hospital; a Botanic Garden; a convention centre next to a river; and a suburb or street named Fitzroy or Flinders.

There's something content and unhurried about Adelaide; it's not a city that strives hard, if at all. The town felt spacious, probably due to its oddly serene lack of passersby. The small number of people reminded me of Dutch towns, and her architecture and expansive sidewalks reminded me of New York City.


Our first stop was lunch at a place called Mandoo, which promised authentic, handmade Korean dumplings—and delivered on that promise. It was run by an elderly couple probably well into their sixties; the man was focused on shaping and folding one dumpling at a time, while the woman flitted to and fro from the kitchen to the counter and then back, never stopping, hands always full. 

My friend and I sat right by the register. When any customer would rise from their tables to pay for their meal, the man and woman exclaimed, Gamsahamnidaa!! with a smile that goes to the apples of their cheeks. Throughout the meal, my friend and I chatted about a myriad of things—such as love and family and careers—enjoying a bowl of udon and a plate of steamed pork dumplings, at the table hosted by the elderly couple with the smiles that lifted the apples of their cheeks. At once it felt less foreign of a city.


We then made our way to the State Library and Art Gallery on the north side of town. At the library, I walked into a dimly lit multi-storey chamber filled with books from floor to ceiling. It was so hushed and ancient that if it weren't for the students sitting around with MacBooks open, it would've felt like stepping into untouched history: mahogany tables, chaise lounges, an old wooden clock whose sounds echoed through the hall: Click, clock, click, clock...

We also stumbled upon a small exhibition recounting the history of Australia's democracy. On a wall, big sign letters read, My voice matters: Does everyone have equal say in Australia? The wall beneath it had slots where you can cast your "vote"—the Yes'es were empty, and the No's were filled to the brim.

I looked closer at a board of colourful post-its next to it, covered in scribbles and handwritings:

"NO. because we took Aboriginal peoples land, and they don't get a say."

"no :("

"Voiceless are the animals."


At the gallery, we floated the way any normal person our age would when visiting a museum or gallery—walking slow enough to appreciate the deeper meaning of each artwork that we'd probably fail to understand, trying to extract sense from a sculpture of needles or an abstract painting to mixed success.

The best part of our first day, we agreed, was when we walked to the gardens and rolled out the picnic mat to sit under the sun. The sun was generous, offering a warm afternoon, and underneath the shade of a native orange tree, we lied down, listened to music, read books, and eventually drifted into a midday sleep.

Day 2

We left the hotel early in search of a breakfast or coffee. The Adelaide Central Market was minutes away, so it wasn't long until we were in the thick of a lively Saturday morning; Adelaide, once hushed and foreign and sparse, was now a plentiful, bustling market hall filled with local weekend chatter, the sounds and smoke of sizzled meats, crates and shopping carts wheeled to and fro, rows of fresh baked pastries, and an ever-lingering smell of coffee.


(We unanimously agreed Melbourne coffee was better.) Still, breakfasts in hand, we wandered through aisles of fresh fruit, seafood, mettwursts which I had to Google the meaning of. In spaces like these, I like to daydream about being the lady who visits the market every weekend, toting a shopping bag, buys a loaf or a baguette, a fresh batch of flowers, and some small random trinket she's running out of like honey or apricot jam, before peacefully making her way home. I see them all the time in markets like these, wearing their own sunhats or effortless, niche pair of hooped earrings. I quietly marvel at the life they must live. 


Later that day, we caught a bus to head to Hahndorf, a quaint, friendly German town (if we could so generously call one strip of a street a town). Amidst a row of shops that sold pretzels, candles, or fudge, my favourite was a Christmas shop decorated with wreaths and giant nutcracker soldiers, though it was only the second day of September.

The German Village Shop had two walls of cuckoo clocks, where a group of families were watching each one in awe. They had sections for children's toys, music boxes, mini wooden cutouts of the nativity scene, and a colourful row of miniature German houses made of clay, painted in intricate detail.

I saw a set of harmonicas in a glass case and thought of my father, who loved the blues and would play these in some random afternoons. When I was a little girl, I remember trying one out, and remember how hard I had to blow just to make a measly sound of a single chord. I wondered how he did it. It occurred to me that Father's Day was the following day.
 
...
 
The afternoon grew later, and I asked my friend if we could catch sunset by the river. Which brings us there, back to the River Torrens, watching couples and families, toddlers in prams, young students walking with their phones held upwards to show the view to their families back home. Party boats carried groups of friends singing to ABBA as they sailed westward on the water. The sound floated further away, and at the sheer beauty and ordinariness of it all, something in my heart lifted.
 

In moments of pause like this, my little gremlin tends to wonder, Do I deserve this? Do I deserve a vacation?

Which made me think of deservingness. We live in such a way where even in moments of pause, we question whether we should be pausing. We begin to believe feeling happy or at peace has to do with how much we've done or how well we've done it.

But as I watch the man with the bike or the two ladies humming a song with the ukulele or the couple holding hands or the jogger running their final lap, it wasn't deservingness that was unfolding in the scene before me, but undefined grace. Either one of us—the little humans in the painting of this scenery—could've told a painful lie, had our hearts crushed, crushed another's heart, made a mistake at work that felt like it was the end of our lives...

And yet. And yet, and yet. Here we all are, graced by the same beautiful afternoon, with the same amount of time and space, and it tasted like forgiveness. It tasted like the world outside of this little bubble doesn't really matter. The buildings slowly turned into a peachy hue. The sky soon turned to violet. My surroundings reminded me of a simple truth: That you can hit all the right buttons and the thing you want doesn't come. You can make a series of missteps and still land somewhere good. The journey to accept this is both hopeful and difficult, because we're trained to believe that our merit is what guarantees us a good life—and it's easier to believe this, because that means we are in full control of every outcome.

But it isn't, and we aren't. And thank goodness we aren't, because most of us are quite imperfect and fractured, if you think about it. There's only the life we build from the hands we're dealt. There is only sheer luck and grace, and yes unfortunately, occassionally, terrible misfortune, and the journey to learn how to accept and carry both.

So I sat on that hill and looked at the glimmering water. I ponder on the three-quarters of a year that has passed and figure there were probably times where I did deserve better, and times that I actually took for granted or deserved far less. Choosing to release and accept that reality, also released me from either ends of palpable hurt and rage, or endless guilt. Sometimes, neither the good nor the bad is in our doing. It's okay to let go of that burden.

So I did. I summoned every unfulfilled "what if" or every demanding "I should've". I cast it to that glimmering water, let it go, and imagined it floating away westward.
