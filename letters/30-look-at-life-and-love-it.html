30. Look at life and love it

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Saturday, 11 February 2023.

My Dear Friend,

Someone rang my intercom at around 9 in the morning. I put down the dishes I was washing, and said hi to him over the speaker, "Yes I'll let you up!"

"Thank you," he said nicely. His name is James, and he was the locksmith assigned to help me fix the balcony lock in my apartment that day.

A late-middle-aged man wearing a dark blue cap and a grey button shirt with lock & lock locksmith stitched above his pocket. Minutes later, I was opening the door.

"Hello!" he bellowed, before I let him in.

"Good morning!" I answered. "How are you?"

"Good, good– You know I used to have a cousin, who lived right on this street!" he said, eyes widened. "In a house, right here!"

"Oh no way!"

"Yes! That was back in, uh, the 1960s or so. Now they're all buildings that just go up and up."

That's how I learned James was a man of stories. I was his first customer of the day, and I ushered him to the lock on my balcony door, that wouldn't budge ever since I moved in.

And you might be thinking, Jo... what thief would go in through a balcony on the 20th floor?

But... you never know.

"Yeah you're my first one today!" he explained. "I'm heading to The Alfred after this, I got a heart transplant."

Oh! I said. The Alfred Hospital is only a 5-minute drive away. Damn it, what do you ask to someone who's had a heart transplant??

"Um..... are you alright?"

"Yeah I'm good! I'm good. It was 13 years ago," he said. And for some reason I sighed, relieved.

"Got an appointment later today, so you know, thought I'd start earlier." Yeah fair enough, I say. I didn't ask about the transplant itself as I didn't want to probe.

I thought of going back to my dishes, or my work, but I didn't want to be rude. So I just lingered in the kitchen, awkwardly, the way you wait on a technician or plumber doing their work. All the while James is pulling out screws from the balcony door, shaking his head at it saying, "These apartments — terrible design!"

"I know," I said. But truthfully I'm none the wiser about door lock design.

I engaged in conversation again. "So just a checkup at the Alfred?"

Yes! He explains it's his start-of year appointment, and how he goes every 6 months. The many tests, including blood tests, "and an exercise test which I'm not looking forward to," he said.

We laughed. "Because they tell you to do lots of things?" I asked?

"Oh, you know before this I used to love running!" James replied. "I loved it. Fit as ever, I was! But I think this person," and he pointed to his heart, "this person doesn't like it as much! So since then it's like my body would just go No, no way."

When he said it like this I couldn't imagine. Having a piece of someone else inside you for life? I imagine what bonds that must bring – for a heart to change masters.

James went on about his work and I was still leaning against my kitchen countertop. He told me about the issue of why the door was jammed, went down to his van and up again to grab extra tools, complained about the property owners some more. He told me about the "French Spiderman", a man who's been climbing all these monuments and buildings without harnesses, without ropes, and has been arrested so many times.

"Yeah look him up on YouTube after this!" he said. "And you can lock the door, 'cause he's still out there."

At the end, he showed me how to close it, and it was fixed. I felt a tinge of curiosity wondering what got James into locksmithing, but it was the end of our time, so I led him on the way out.

I felt it wasn't the end of the conversation, though, so I asked him the same thing I asked Nikola the Uber driver, whom I once wrote to you about.

"Well lastly, actually!" I said as he opened the door, "what advice would you give for young people?"

I figured the best case scenario was I learn something, and the worst case was he would leave thinking I was strange. (Which I can live with.)

James raised his eyebrows, seeming taken aback. "Uh, you mean for like locks and stuff?"

"No, just life in general!"

"Oh! Um!" and he looked to the sun, through the balcony door that he was fixing.

"You know, enjoy life," he said.

"Enjoy life, laugh... you know, even I'm — I'm singing when I'm shopping! You know what I mean? And people look at me and go, what's this crazy man- I'm not going near him, but– you know?? Enjoy life. Look at life and love it. Some people, they're a hermit, and they stay at home... It's great to stay at home! But, make sure you enjoy things. Enjoy what you do. Like for me, it's music. I love music.

"And, uh, be a positive person. Stay positive. I'm a positive person, I always have been. It shows everywhere, in your health, on your skin."

He continued, "I'm caring for a lady who's in a wheelchair – she's my sister-in-law — and, uh, she's not a happy person," and this time in his voice there was sadness. James tells me about her failed marriages, her difficult attitude, even about one time in a doctor's waiting room where she was shouting to another family because of the colour of their skin.

The words he used were, relationship collapse. "When a relationship collapses, you got to sit and ask yourself why," he said. "She very set in her ways. And often in a relationship, you gotta just – you gotta bite the bullet. You know? You need to go, Okay. I need to change. And be willing to change."

And James ended his advice by telling me a joke, but for the life of me I can't remember what it was.

But he was still smiling and laughing as he left. So we wished each other a good day, and I thanked him and closed the door, thinking all the while about the strength of a person's heart.
