Relationships, as taught by cinema

by Joanne Amarisa
Bali, Indonesia. Wednesday, 1 June 2022.
 

My Dearest Friend,

I've been waking up to the sound of roosters again, and it reminds me of the camping trips I used to go to growing up. The green is lush, the grass is always a little damp. I'm surrounded by palm trees, and each time I stare at lapping waves I am left in a daze. 

Small life update: I have been in Bali this past week reuniting with my family. It has felt surreal, after the past 2 years, to have made plans and then actually witness and experience them come to life. Even stepping into an aircraft and waking up elsewhere — what a new feeling! While I am here, I deploy all my senses to record and savour everything. It's a whole "now or never" kind of living now, so I've been clinging onto every memory for dear life.

There are so many ways this island makes me wax poetic, and there's so much I can tell you. Maybe I'll start with one thing. 

Being back in the presence of family, now that I'm home with them after so long of being apart, has made me think, extensively, about relationships. It's not just because the number one thing most people seem to be curious about is whether or not I'm dating anyone (oh, mercy) but because I am now observing all bonds held with my loved ones — in familial relationships or older friendships — in a new light.

There is a YouTube channel I like to watch called Cinema Therapy. Fitting that therapy is in the name, as it offers me a similar comforted feeling on reflective moments like these. The show is run by two men by the name of Jonathan Decker and Alan Seawright — Jonathan is a "therapist who loves movies" and Alan, in his own words, is a "filmmaker and I need therapy." They sit together in a makeshift home theatre to watch and analyse movie scenes, covering everything from characters to filmmaking technique, thanks to their combined expertise.

This idea hooked me from the get-go, because I love psychoanalysing movie frames, and because Jonathan & Alan do a great job of debunking cinematic tropes and squeezing every bit of nuance to turn into applicable lessons in life and relationships. Lessons I can use, especially as I perpetually recalibrate myself to settle into these family and friendship dynamics. May I share with you two I kept in mind?


Shelf your agenda: This is a theory from Dr. John Gottman as written in his book, The Relationship Cure. Shelving Your Agenda is a conflict resolution technique, which Dr. Gottman describes as this...

"The skill of temporarily bypassing our natural defensiveness, desire to explain ourselves, and urge to correct or question someone else, in favour of being present for the needs of another in the moment."

We put our needs on the shelf. They will come off the shelf later, but for now they’re up there so we can be there for someone else. The perfect movie depiction of this is Kristoff, our reindeer-loving musical icon of Frozen 1 & 2. It's not mentioned explicitly, but he demonstrates it the entire length of the film. He's searching for a way to ask Anna to marry him, he's uncomfortable that everyone is headed towards danger (as is the premise of every Disney film), but he constantly shelves his agenda to support Anna in her time of need.

The spotlight of this is when Kristoff swoops in to save Anna in one of the final scenes of Frozen 2. The first thing he says is not "You're welcome", or "What's going on? What did you do?" Instead his line was: "I'm here, what do you need?"

Because I have been learning for the past couple of years the hard skill of standing up for myself, and expressing what I need or feel, being told to shelf my agenda feels counterintuitive. I am naturally vocal and protective (even defensive, sometimes) of my needs and am rarely welcome to the idea of them being dismissed or put aside. But shelving your agenda is not a matter of bottling your feelings; which is actually stoicism and conflict avoidance. In contrast, it acknowledges that our feelings, perspectives, and questions do matter and will be addressed, but we take the hard turn and instead practice empathy, patience, and showing up.

A family member may do something I cannot tolerate, or we may find ourselves at the crux of disagreement — as blissful as my trip back home has been, this will happen — shelving my agenda means being okay with not addressing my own needs at this very second, so I can be present for the other person or keep them from hurting. Not to people-please, but simply because if it comes down to the relationship or my ego, the relationship needs saving first.
 

Be direct: Now comes the moment where we do speak, and this is where we enter more... inflammable territory.

In an animated film called The Mitchells vs The Machines (2021), there is a teenage protagonist Riley who struggles to see eye-to-eye with her dad: a textbook overly protective father. He doesn't understand her ambitions, she feels he doesn't care, they both fail to communicate, and the rest will sound familiar. (They're also fighting the robot apocalypse here, but that's obviously less important.)

Whether you live on opposite ends of the world or sitting next to each other on the dining table, I realise being an adult is about being confronted with the need to build a bridge in order to understand your loved ones. I may move away and find that my loved ones carry different ideals or views of the world than the ones I formed on my own, away from them. What must one do then?

Jonathan of Cinema Therapy brings an excellent point in relation to this: In conflict or disagreement, when we don't want to hurt anyone's feelings, we start to wring ourselves inside out to try and find the right words to say. This is where miscommunication happens. When we put on our people-pleasing armour, we'd get caught up in trying to either sound nice, or be right, when in fact the real main message that mustn't get lost is: "Despite our differences, I still love and care for you, and this relationship still matters."

Expressing this doesn't have to be complicated. Jonathan suggests, practice saying: "I want you to feel loved, seen, and respected. But I don't know how to meet that need. How can I be a better friend*?" Sister? Brother? Partner? Parent? I cringe as I type this, so you may cringe as you read it, but what we need to learn is the importance of being direct. It's the more vulnerable route, but it's the one that douses the flame. In Frozen for instance, I think one of the reasons their bond was so strong was because of how expressive Anna was (constantly reiterating, "I don't want to see you get hurt!" or "I don't want to lose you!") 

If you want to meet somewhere on that bridge, both parties need to establish that there is a bridge, and that it's not going anywhere. Establish it at the start before it gets heated, establish it at the end when the debate finds no common ground. At the end of the day and no matter who earns the 'rightness', if you ask sincerely how you can love another person better, 90% of the time, people will have an answer for you. 

Tell me: When have you had to shelf your agenda? In what area of your life would you like to be more direct?


PS: There were more lessons from the show specifically on love and dating, grief, and worldview which I couldn't fit into this one letter, so I shall leave you with this for now. (Plus I still struggle to write about dating without feeling unqualified and iffy!) Once I crystallise more thoughts and poignant nuggets of wisdom, I'll write to you some more. In the meantime, I hope the week is treating you kindly.
