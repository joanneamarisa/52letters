15. All my love, all my fears

by Joanne Amarisa
Surabaya, Indonesia. Friday, 29 July 2022.

My Dear Friend,

Do you remember when I wrote to you a few months ago, about how, leading up to my departure from Melbourne, my weeks were full of savouring? Well today, I'm three weeks away from returning to Melbourne and I'm experiencing similar effects, tenfold.

First of all, I must apologise for not writing to you sooner. Three days after my brother's wedding, my temperature spiked and I was bedridden with a fever and cough for days. Every test came back negative which was strange, but now — a week later — four of my family members tested positive. So here I am, floating in limbo as one of the negative odd ones out in the family. Grateful, if not cautious and a little puzzled as I occupy a half-empty home.

Being ever so mellow, when I was down with a fever, I scrolled through photos on my gallery to pass the time. Glimpses of my first month at home include: A straw hat I bought on a whim in Bali. Clouds as seen from an airplane window seat. My brother in a half-finished wedding suit, with loose ends coming out of his pocket sleeve and pin needles where stitchings would be. 

A couple weeks ago, I shed a tear the moment I saw him walk down the aisle. Donned in the best looking suit I've ever seen him wear, with a dashing green tie we picked out a few days prior.

The witnessing of a milestone set my brain gears in motion — how quickly life moves forward. How easily time escapes us.
 
...

If you ask me what has been running through my mind these final few weeks at home, here is what I would answer:

The way my back aches from too much laughter. There was a moment after the wedding where we were gathered as a big family — and we joked about everything from kids to wedding guests to my aunts and uncles' shenanigans during the ceremony. There are snacks on the coffee table, and a faint smell of cigarettes, and we laugh, and laugh, and laugh, and the memory fills me with a love that could last me many years.

Witnessing my brother and his new wife, and also my sister and her partner. How lucky I feel to have "teachers" of what a healthy love looks like: Laughing at, and laughing with. Giving generous room to grow. Letting each other's strengths and weaknesses meet, and form a team.

The way my mother took care of me when I was ill. The Asian parent love language of supermarket fruits and heated bone broth, and other natural remedies we city kids have never heard of. How my mother gives, and gives, and gives, to each and every one of us without question. Some of my new core memories with her include sitting in the car, watching her drive while she taps her hands on the steering wheel when I put on Glenn Miller's In the Mood.

Yet of course, families are only people. And people are rarely perfect. Therefore, sharing a life is never only sunshine and rainbows.

There are also the mundane moments; sitting around dinner tables with nothing to say, bearing the tantrums of my sister's toddlers. And then the low moments: Witnessing with your own two eyes the challenges your family goes through, most of which are out of your control. (Annoying, isn't it? To be "adult" yet still lack the universe's control?)

A few weeks ago, I saw the Instagram story of a friend in Melbourne who had her family coming over. "I forgot what it was like to not come home to an empty apartment," she mentioned, "and to be able to simply be yourself." Her words, along those lines, made me ponder. I related so strongly that I wondered, Have I... not been myself all this time?

Here is what it feels like: When you live in a foreign country, you're in a "fend for yourself" mode. I think that's more of what she meant. You carve a sort of independence and life becomes, by definition, growing apart from everything you know, and growing into a more rational, a more individualised shell of a self.

Living at home, albeit temporarily, has thrown me into the opposite end. I learn of individual and collective pains we promise to shoulder together. I adopt a complete paradigm shift and learn what it means to co-exist, love, forgive, share in each other's burdens. Counterintuitively, when the time calls for it, I practice knowing when not to put myself first.

To soak in the gift of knowing your loved ones — to enjoy the privilege of being a part of their lives — without also journeying with them through their lows and embracing their imperfections, would be to love them incompletely.

Loving deeply is to stretch your heart wide open. To excavate into it far and deep. Once you've done that, anything can fill its caverns; both love and pain, both affection and frustration. But traveling to both ends is worth it. The right people have a way of making it worth it.
 
...

On the day of my brother's wedding, I woke an hour earlier so I could stop by his room. At the crack of dawn, the morning of a wedding always feels like a hushed chaos. A dimmed hotel room, the soft shutters of a camera, the buzzing neon lights from the makeup artist's table. The room smells of fresh flowers, with a hint of brewed coffee. Both excitement and nerves are palpable, like the simmering of a kettle before it reaches the boil. 

Everyone's a little nervous, a little on edge, especially my brother in these first seconds of his day as a groom. A few days prior, I'd seen him pace back and forth in our living room, memorising and writing and rewriting his wedding vows.

A lot can emerge in wedding planning; disagreements, anxieties, the occasional COVID scares... The point, perhaps, is not a day of flawless scenarios. The point, perhaps, is love and presence. Both the offering and the receiving. 

That morning, I spent an unremarkable 20 minutes with my brother, simply just to be there. I didn't offer anything particularly memorable, only making small talk in a bid to break the ice and shake his nerves away.

I realise I don't want to miss a minute. I don't want to miss the things that could excite, nor devastate me. Both on that day, and in life with my family in general.

When I say family, we may think of blood relations within the confines of some arbitrary family tree. But I consider that family is whomever I can show all my love, all my fears, with the space wide enough in their hearts to hold it. They are those who recognise our faults, yet love us unconditionally. Whose faces come to mind? Isn't it true that we choose families too? The parent with whom I share my breakfasts, the friend I can talk about anything to, the two roommates in Melbourne who, by odd and wondrous miracle, have become more like my sisters.

The least we can offer to those who matter to us is presence. The least we can receive from them, regardless of whether or not we feel deserving of it, is love.

I pack my bags this weekend, and I'm less preoccupied nowadays with defining where home is. Instead, I take comfort in being stretched between many. I decide that home is wherever a love like this can find me, freely.
