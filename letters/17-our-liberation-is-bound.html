17. Our liberation is bound

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. 17 August 2022.

My Dear Friend,

On this 77th Independence Day of my motherland, it seems less like a letter I'm writing to you. It feels like a letter I can write for centuries — like this country, my feelings towards it resemble unfinished stories.


I.

Perhaps we start here: At the end of my three-month trip. Last Friday, a set of plane wheels took off, sending me miles away, once again, from the land I call home.

Land is the first way I identify her. Not her air, filled with smoke and sawdust. Nor her water, which ranges from clear blue oceans to murky brown swamplands.

But her land. When I came to Australia four years ago, it was the difference of land I noticed first. Compared to dry gum trees, Indonesia's foliage is lush. So unkempt, so unconcerned with who takes care of it, yet always thriving, somehow.

A land that has gone through so much, has been mined and burnt and carved out hollow. But dig your hands in any forest, and you will find damp, brown soil. A ground that clings to raindrops. It will leave muddy prints on your calves and it will give you water should you ask. It still crumbles, like cookie dough, with a gentle press. Notice the way it easily forms shoeprints. Notice the ways she would give you anything.

My most frequent memories of encountering this soil was school trips, holding hands with friends as we make our way to nearby waterfalls. This land doesn't give us drinkable tap water, but it gives us waterfalls, that stand majestic and fall abundant. They seem to look down from their cliffs unto us and say, "You think I needed your help?"

So futile, so flawed are the ways we try to protect this land. Not recognising, often, that it is she who protects us.


II.

Some things I consider quintessential to life on this land: Morning dew. The sound of roosters. A foggy morning to a smoky dusk. Roaring motorcycles. Prayers from mosques. Food stalls (what they lack in amenities, they make up for in flavour.)

Many will tell you this country is unfit to live in. I say, it is unwelcome to those who want it easy.

It's as beautiful as it is brutal. As hospitable as it is unforgiving. Take Jakarta, for example, our least liveable, most loveable capital. To move to Jakarta is to want more. The first drive you take down Sudirman, you'll feel the skyscrapers staring down your neck. "Who's the newcomer?" they seem to ask.

Polluted air blankets the skyline. Faint jazz plays in the hotel lobbies, and click-clacks of high heeled shoes echo across marble floors. (In this city, money lingers mysteriously.) Rows of vehicles light the streets red come 6 PM; those in the driver seats dream about dinner, and the feeling of coming home.


III.

On September 19th 1945, there was an incident on the rooftop of Hotel Yamato. A ripping sound, onlookers testified. Two young men had teared the blue stripe off the Netherlands flag — angrily, and rightfully so.

By that time, our land was free, making the Dutch flag illegal (and wildly offensive) to hoist. As that stripe of blue falls to the ground, I'd like us to consider how that freedom happened.

On August 16th, a month prior, four other young men woke up at 4 in the morning with a plan. To kidnap the nation's leaders to force them to sign a document that declares this country ours. A rebellious tale, although many of the stories that fill our history textbooks are. So early before the sun even shone, they went to a secluded house in Western Java.

Photos show them lounging on rattan armchairs, porcelain cups sitting on the coffee table. At a glance it would have seemed normal, like a family get-together, with frames on walls and flowers in the foyer. But history would tell us, they were making a case for freedom. Japan had just surrendered in the Pacific War. The officials hesitated, but the young people didn't want to wait any longer. 

Freedom now or freedom never, they argued, if we don't seize it now, we fall back into their hands. 

They were right. Because a month later, the Dutch and Japanese returned to claim a power that was no longer theirs. Which brings us back to the ripped flag on the rooftop of Hotel Yamato that hot September day. And to the wars and street fights that later ensued.

This was the medium of independence: Young people. Radio stations. Megaphones. Blood.

This was the morning of independence: Citizens walking out of their homes to find morning light falling on empty streets. The Japanese soldiers had gone. 


IV.

Decades after the fights took place on the streets of Surabaya, a peace settled over the city. Alleys that once saw wounds is now lined with shaded trees. Hotels that were once barricades, now remodelled to their original purpose.

It's the same city I happened to grow up in. A little over a month ago, we celebrated my brother's wedding at the Hotel Yamato (now Majapahit). When you're Indonesian, the story of independence entwines with everything you love, whether you like it or not.

That story is about a land that continues to give, even after centuries of injustice. How generous of her, to be granting us new trees after giving in for hundreds of years to the footsteps of the Dutch.

It's about my grandmother's cooking, my mother's smile, my grandfather's wind chimes — how a hasty independence paved the way for freedom to be ours, too.

And it's about the youths, who fought for freedom in the first place, because what is being young if not seizing what the world is unwilling to hand us?

Which would you choose?, countless people ask me, To stay in Indo or move far away?

I can only tell you that when the plane first landed, I saw glimpses of the land I know. Squared patches of farmland, mountains in the distance, everything lush and empty and awfully familiar.

Yet my love for it exceeds nostalgia. Our liberation is bound. 

My feet have carried me miles, but I deeply suspect, my heart had never travelled that far.

 

Dalam semua megah dan rupamu, dengan semua ketidaksempurnaanmu,

Dirgahayu Indonesiaku.
