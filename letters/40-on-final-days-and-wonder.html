40. On final days and wonder

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 25th May 2023.

My Dear Friend,

I must've taken so many walks across Fawkner Park that nowadays I float right through it. My feet go from concrete to pebbled path and side-step the puddles thoughtlessly. The trees still leave me breathless like the first time I came here, especially in this last gasp of autumn. Like always, the mothers push their prams, and the runners jog, and the dogs heel, and the elderly couples take their leisurely time.

Then I pass by a school during what must've been recess hour. From the distance, I see kids in uniform scattered across the playground—like a joy explosive had erupted in the middle of the grass.

There is screaming and laughing as they frolicked and swung and rolled and fell and hopped and climbed. A group of boys are collecting rocks into a Ziploc bag. A little girl in a school dress and pigtails spins herself uncontrollably, her head tilted towards the sky. Another young girl wraps her arms around a friend, attempting to lift him off the ground.

I find myself surrounded by all this reckless wonder, and it makes me think about youth—and about mine, in particular.

One of the books I finished this past few weeks was Tuesdays with Morrie. In it, Mitch Albom recounts the Tuesdays he spent visiting his dying professor, Morrie Schwartz. Predictably, they talk about things such as success and marriage and family...

To be honest: We all know what most people say on their deathbeds. To make the most of our youth, to be kind, to realise that relationships and being with the ones you love is the only thing that matters in the end. But I suppose when you're desperately having a bad day, or when you really want to impress your boss, it's difficult for these things to go beyond theory.

As I read through the scenes of Morrie's final days and breath, and think back to the children on the playground, I both laugh, and shudder. I laugh, remembering I, too, was once 4, fighting over who gets to go on the slide first. I, too, was once the angsty age of 15, convinced that everything I was feeling, no one in the world understood.

 
All this reckless wonder

Now at 23, I recognise there's enough space in the world for everybody, and that everything I'm feeling is as universal as it is personal.

Yet I shudder, because perhaps youth is as fast, as slippery, as the world suggests. That like sand, it may escape your grasp if you don't cling to it tightly.

But I want to explore a different meaning. I want to treat my youth as clay. Like playdough, I want these "younger years" to be something I can stretch, and curl up, and mold into anything I want. I want to fold its marbled colours together, letting it mesh into grey goo, only to introduce to it new colours to brighten up again. I want to roll it out, like pizza, letting it reach the furthest edges of my table.

My time is running out—play time isn't forever—so I'd like to have as much fun as I can in the process.

And when I stop looking at my youth like that slipping sand, and instead as this malleable, colourful thing, I feel less of that panic of wondering whether I am on the obscure "right track", or whether I have made myself loveable, successful, worthy enough in the time I have spent.

What can possibly matter as I stand in this hourglass? What can I control, other than the lens through which I view my own life?

What can I do, other than tell the people I love I love them, and keep my joy so well intact, that I may depart this beautiful earth filled with so much wonder?
 

This week I finished reading:
Tuesdays with Morrie by Mitch Albom
Mere Christianity by C.S. Lewis

Now I'm picking up:
Tiny Beautiful Things by Cheryl Strayed
