22. Wealthy and healthy

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 22 September 2022.

My Dear Friend,

I made a new friend this week! Well, maybe not friend... He was the silver-haired gentleman who happened to accept my Uber request one morning. (Sometimes you're just running a little too late, you know?)

Mr. Nikola is one of the chatty drivers, you'll know soon enough. He wore a jacket that I first thought was a suit, which would've given him a dapper chaperone vibe — but no, it was just a very good, vintage camper jacket. An ornament of Jesus (or was it Mother Mary?) hangs from his rearview mirror. When I entered his car, the radio was on. He begins a conversation.

How are you, he asks. Good thank you, how's your morning? I say. Good, good, he replies.

"You can take your mask off, if you'd like," he said. Oh thank you! I said.

"They barely do anything!" he told me. "If you have Covid and we're in this car, there's not much we can do." I politely laugh. 

Sounds minimally cautious for his age, I thought. What is his accent? European? Mr. Nikola drove very safely.

"You know, there was a time," he said, "that you can smoke on airplanes! Can you believe it!" He ends every sentence with a chuckle. And on he talked about airlines, and their no-smoking policy, and how it used to be 'back in the days'.

"Did you watch the Queen's funeral last night?" he asked.

"I did!" I said. My mother did not want to miss it, Sir, I wanted to add.

"It's crazy, I've just seen the Queen a week before that," he said, "still standing, smiling and shaking hands with people. A week later, she's gone!" He chuckles again. 

Italian, I think. That must be it. 

Wait, like see her in person? Or did he mean the TV? Surely on the TV. Oh well, too late to ask now.

"But she did look quite frail. You know I have a friend... 93 years old, a more active gentleman than Her Majesty. Every time I see him, he wants to buy me coffee. I say Let me buy you one and he says No Nik, I buy you coffee!" And on he chatted — about his friend's generosity, the sudden news of his passing away. As we were nearing traffic along Punt Road, the sun peeked through, indicating a warm sunny day in Melbourne.

He told me to guess his age, and I was about 9 years off the correct answer, and we laughed. "I'm the youngest in my club!" he says with a laugh. His sprightly-ness says it all. At one point he looked at me from the rearview and said, "You're still young! Have a long way to go!"

And then I think to myself: You can learn from him, you know. Now or never.

"So what advice would you give, then?" I finally said, "For the young people?"

I let it hang in the air, just like that. He smiled as he was thinking.

"Worry as little as possible," he told me, with full conviction. "Worrying — it's not going to fix anything. It's going to make you sick."

Timely. You probably know by now, dear friends & readers, that I'm quite the worrier. In that car, even, I was half in the conversation and half worrying about being late. He was right, of course — if worrying had worked in fixing things, our lives would've been perfect by now, no?

"And second thing is," he added, "try to do things. Go out and do things."

And I thought, okay, like try all the things and do all the jobs and stuff right? He's going to talk about how he became an Uber driver?

But he said, "My friend always says: To be wealthy and healthy, help others as much as possible."

He continued, "A lot of people are selfish, we're all thinking about ourselves and our problems. But try to give, and help others, you know? Even in the Bible, it says it is more blessed to give, than to receive."

Amen.

Well there you go :)
