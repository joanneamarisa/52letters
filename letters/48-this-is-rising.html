December 16, 2023

48. This is rising

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Sunday, 17 December 2023.

My Dear Friend,

Good morning. I fell asleep in the living room, and as I woke, a strip of morning light was on the wall opposite of me. I had forgotten how much I loved mornings. I looked out past the balcony and caught a glimpse of sunrise, and it gave me a sliver of hope again after a dark night.

We are soon approaching my second Christmas card letter to you—and if you are wondering, yes, my letters are behind schedule, as "52" was intended to fulfil each week of the year. But we will forgive ourselves and make do. There's been a lot of that this year.

Heavy hearts are made heavier in Christmas time—I'm not trying to be a grinch, but it does seem to be the reality. And perhaps like some of you, I am trying to navigate the direness of it all. 

But I encourage you, especially as Christmas draws nearer and nearer, that if you're approaching the holidays with a different capacity—if rather than a "bang", your end of the year feels more like holding on, that that's okay too. Even if that means holding on to the littlest things like the way a sunrise looks one morning. It's okay to feel like a wounded lamb being carried to the finish line. Treat yourself that gently, if that's what your heart needs.

It's a challenging time of year because sometimes going through something that isn't quite "cheerful" becomes aggravated with a pressure of needing to be okay, happy, and festive by Christmas time. And so my dearest Friend, you brave, resilient fighter and amazing human you, take this letter as the sign that you can take that pressure off.


Buying supermarket flowers

How am I holding onto hope? I wouldn't consider this a guide as much as it is a reminder to myself, so maybe we'll recount these together and you can share me what you do too. 

I am learning that rising looks different for everybody and every day. To me, it's sometimes things that seem trivial, selfish, and a little stupid. One of them is buying $15 bouquets of supermarket flowers that will exist in my living room for a good few weeks. 

Last night, I cooked an egg. The day before that, I hung my laundry and finished doing the dishes. The other night, it was a homemade soft tofu stew. This morning, it's this letter. 

Your oasis will look different on different days, but it's important to actively seek it and revel in it when you find one. Take it hour by hour, bask in the smallest victories.

Narrowing my Google Calendar view

If the choice comes down between you and an errand, choose you. If it even comes down between you and a deadline, choose you. If it can wait, let it wait. If it can wait and the world doesn't crash and burn, let it wait. 

For me, I've made the simple switch of narrowing my calendar view from 1 Week to 3 Days. I'm not sure why, but it helps me focus more on the present—rather than mind-mapping what each week holds. At any given time, I'm forced to focus on only yesterday, today, and tomorrow. Or today, tomorrow, and the day after that. Pull in the reins and restrain yourself from things that may add to the overwhelm, however simple the change.

It's manifested in other ways, too. I've started to say things like, "Okay, that's for Monday Joanne," or, "January Joanne will know what to do." It slows me down and extends some grace to who I am at the present moment—the one who might need it most. Monday or January me may not end up loving that. But I think they'd appreciate the trust.

Gratitude, truth, gratitude, truth.

Seek the goodness in all that's happened this year and clutch those moments tightly to your chest. As the saying goes, "There is always something to be grateful for." My therapist suggests the gratitude diary where you list three things you're grateful for each morning. I whisper them to myself most times, even though I tend to gravitate towards the same three things day to day. (Someone I love, someone I love, and someone I love.)

But another thing I've found helpful, other than gratitude, is truth. Un-truths tend to imply haste, worry, or self-demand: "Have I done enough this year? Am I falling behind? Will next year be better? I should be doing XYZ..."

You gotta nip that in the bud, sweet pea. Remember, we're being gentle and nice to ourselves for the remainder of this year. That's a promise we're keeping.

So the challenge but also the mandate every single day is to bring those thoughts into the light of facts: I have people whom I know love me and are there for me. I have time and I will heal, and no feeling is eternal. Nothing is ever as definitive or catastrophic to my life as I imagine it to be. I am learning. I'll know better in a few months' time than I do right now, and it's okay for things to take time. 

Allow yourself to be anchored by a few of those words; a few of the things you're grateful for and then a few of your own truths. Let yourself be the tattered ship, staying afloat while waiting for the waves and the storm to pass.

I'm holding out hope for us that it will. And who knows, maybe joy will meet us right here.

You and I have some work to do, and that work is we have to keep going.

Though the year ends soon, your journey is still far and long. You are allowed hope. You are allowed time. You are allowed to reinvent yourself any time you're ready. (And it doesn't have to be January 1st.)
 

What is a truth you're holding onto? What has given you joy lately?
