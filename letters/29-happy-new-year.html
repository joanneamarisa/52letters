29. Happy new year?

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Sunday, 29 January 2023.

My Dearest Friend,

I spent a lot of time going back and forth about what to write to you, commemorating our first letter since the new year began. (How long until we are no longer allowed to say new year? Because there are chances I might say it until June.)

Every year around this time, I am nostalgic, excited, and a mixed bag of jittery anxiety. I mentally say to the clock that wouldn't stop ticking, Oh my God, slow down! I'm not ready for change! Can I do it later? I try and bargain for the comfort of December again, when newness was imminent but not so face-to-face with me yet.

In my inbox, James Clear was giving his tips on forming new habits. On my Instagram feed, I read through friends' poignant, introspective captions that reflected on 2022 and set intentions for 2023. For a while, I felt like taking it all in. Absorbing all this hope floating in the air, pocketing some for the days that turn dreary. 

That's right — I'm already thinking about the days that will turn dreary. Not only that, I brace myself for them. Parallel with hope, my mind concocts a list of scenarios of what could go wrong within the year, just so I stay prepared.

I realised recently, through Brené Brown's book Daring Greatly, that this habit is called "foreboding joy". In the book, Brené wrote about her surprise when she asked research participants what moments made them feel the most vulnerable.

It would be expected for them to answer: during a test, at a job interview... But a number of them answered that they felt most exposed when they felt hope and was joyful: Getting engaged, welcoming a newborn, getting promoted, liking their job, falling in love.

So for those people — *cough* me — emerging joy or excitement is soon followed with: Well something might still mess up, right? There's still a chance I might fail? We think that if we could see disappointment coming — if we could outwit it — the impact would hurt us less. 

It doesn't, of course. I know I don't want to live that way anymore. Instead, I'd like to revive my sense of wonder, expecting for the year to be good, even if that means being let down on occasion. Because believing and expecting goodness actually gives us more strength to endure through the bad, not less.

So how do we revive that wonder? How do we stop armouring up, and let ourselves feel fuller joy this year?


1. Increasing our resilience to failure.

A friend asked me towards the end of last year, "What's one lesson that you learned this year that you think is worth sharing?"

My answer was, that trying and failing is not the worst thing that could happen to you.

Replace failure with heartbreak, sadness, or disappointment, and we all have either felt it, or lived in fear of it. I received more rejection letters in 2022 than in any other year. And I laugh as I look back at it; I think there should be an art exhibition someday, where we display all our rejection letters, in all their heart-wrenching glory. (Anyone in?)

My question wasn't around why, but how. When my doubtful inner critic questioned, "Have I become lazy? Is it because I didn't try hard enough?" My more rational self jokingly argued, "Straight-A lifelong people-pleaser like you? Never!"

So what was it?

I realised it's because when that first "no" came in, I survived it. And then I realised, Oh. We do survive failure.

From then, I became somewhat "addicted" to trying. Without fear of what the outcome would do to you, it enables you to keep going, applying, asking, trying, with full stubborn belief that you could do it. 

Honestly, it's almost like playing dumb. These days, I don't give my imposter syndrome time to fully form, and pay very little attention to my inner critic's theories. Both I do on purpose. What we think about ourselves is interesting information, but isn't always true.

I'm more drawn to the miraculous, transformative thing that happens when my priority is always to give myself a chance: I'm less fixated on result. Less ashamed if I don't win. In fact, given we've done our best, and have learned something from the process, I wouldn't consider it a failure. When you put yourself out there, you've won over the fear by not letting it keep you from trying. 

Regardless of outcome, I have never regretted trying. Even though it's hard, takes time, work, and a bucket load of vulnerability. And if some days I'm proven wrong, and inevitably I will be, my heart remains light. My resilience builds. And my pride & ego– well actually, those don't survive the process at all. But I think that is the whole point.


2. Believing people when they say (or show) they love us 

Here's the thing: We know that in every person's life, no matter how generous and kind-hearted they are, everyone has the people they are most to least closest to. We have varying levels of trust, shared interest, time spent — it's only natural, and even healthy.

But our problem is, we become adults, and our interpersonal relationships become more calculated and self-critical. We worry about where we fall within these ranks. Are we close that way? Would it be weird if I asked for help or to hang out? Am I being too much? Pair that with the fear of "showing all our cards too soon", and we construct a habit & culture of keeping each other at arm's length.

I realised I wasn't the only one feeling this way about navigating adult friend/relationships. Earlier last week, my friend and I sat on the front steps of our other friend's apartment. And we both spoke about how massive our circles felt in university, and how it has dwindled naturally over time.

In contrary to school when timelines were linear and everyone was friends by proximity, adult relationships come and go, making it hard to discern where we sit on other people's "scales". But I'm tired of living on eggshells – of second guessing my friendships. 

Especially the ones where I know they bring good value to my life, I know they want to see me happy and okay, and I know they're friends with whom I can be honest and clear with, including about boundaries.

People agreeing to be a part of your life, and allowing you to be a part of theirs, is the gift. What if we embrace, even assume mutual love and care, without worrying about how much or how little those "portions" are?

Someone on Twitter said, "I would never ask a friend to help me move. I'm an adult. Hire movers and save a friendship." 

And to that, I'd like to write to all my friends reading this: You can ask me to hold your hand at the dentist, and I would. You can show up at my door, unannounced, and sit on my couch telling me a story, unguarded. In case it hasn't been said, you are allowed to need other people. You are not too much. If they've chosen to be part of your life, they care about you. There's no catch, there's no litmus test. That's it.

As written in Natasha Lunn's Conversations on Love, we need to get better at recognising the love we have, instead of being caught up on the love we fear we don't.

My advice isn't perfect for the new year, because I'm just as confused as you are — but because we're friends (at least, in the letter-sending category???) my only hope is that the next time someone says to you or shows that they're there for you, that you don't question it.

That you simply believe them, say thank you, and let yourself be loved.
