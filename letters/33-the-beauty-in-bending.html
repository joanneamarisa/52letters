33. The beauty in bending

by Joanne Amarisa

Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 10 March 2023.

My Dear Friend,

I was sitting on the second level of a double-decker bus yesterday, driving towards the city after spending the day with a friend. I was reading the last pages of my book, when I decided to look up and out the window. We were riding along the bends of the Yarra River, the water glinting, and the city's silhouettes framing my view in the distance. 

The sun sank low, and it reached my face and hands, all awash in its warm glow. I squinted, to see the rowers making their way home. And I felt only peace, wishing the bus would go a little more slowly.

If you have caught up with me at any moment in the past 6 months, you might be familiar with my flight risk. Basically, up until mid-January, I was convinced it was time for me to leave and fly home for good.

"I'll probably move soon," I said to friends and family, with only a teaspoon of conviction each time.

The plan was March. It's March now, and there are dinner ingredients in my kitchen, and fresh laundry on the drying rack. It doesn't look like I'm going anywhere.

In January, I landed a new job that I didn't expect to get. So after throwing in the towel, I've had to awkwardly pick it back up, and re-enter the phase of: Okay God... what now? 

I thought we had a plan! I debate. I mapped it all out and it was going to be easy.

Whose plan? He asks.

And that kind of shut me up for a good few weeks.
 
...
 
This letter is about learning how to bend, and what to do when you have no choice but to relinquish that control.

I'm not going to do a psychoanalysis into why we have a need for control. I just know that most of us have it, in one way or another — a hyper-vigilance that may reflect in your work, your relationships, personal life, parenting, etc. For some of us, it shows up more often than others. The psychologists I follow say it could be perfectionism, attachment issues, result of trauma...

(But psshh.. it's not like you and I ever struggle with any of these things, right?)*

*For the avoidance of doubt, this is sarcasm.
 

We want so badly to set the terms to how our life will look like, because uncertainty is an uncomfortable, vulnerable feeling, for which mapping out life with our own logic is a very convenient remedy.

But you and I both know it doesn't work that way. The promotion goes to someone else, the relationship doesn't work, the application falls through... And so for a lot of us, we are endlessly wrestling with our life, contorting it to fit the shapes and moulds we've expected and designed for it.  

Enter: grace. And serendipity, and the simple possibility that something can be wildly unexpected, beyond your cleverest schemes, yet still be lasting and good.

Brené Brown says in her book, "Our need for certainty in [uncertain endeavours] makes strategies both seductive and dangerous. Because certainty often breeds absolutes, intolerance, and judgment."

For example, this became apparent when I was helping my family plan their trip to Melbourne later in April. At one point, I told off my brother for making changes to our existing plans or schedules — which I had neatly typed into my Notes app. I called his changes "last minute" when, in hindsight, this was 8 weeks before the trip. And on I whined to him about my time off, and the weather forecasts, and the bookings we have to reschedule (even though those were harmless changes).

Look, God gave me a patient brother for this very reason. But this is why that line in the book was also very timely.

Over something as simple as a shift in my original plans, I became someone I didn't want to be, but also someone I recognised: Irritable, unwilling to bend, majorly freaks out when something isn't to plan.

This is a trivial example, of course, but it has reminded me this past few weeks (and perhaps for the rest of the year) about the importance of learning to bend.

We do not set the terms. I don't think that was ever in the deal. When we enter the world, we don't enter with a guarantee that we'll always know exactly what awaits us 6 or 12 months ahead. (Which sounds incredibly boring?) 

Or that we'll always know what's ahead if we're just smart enough, careful enough, or can become good enough in planning. (Like, that might be great, but also, how stressful to live that way!)

We all don't. None of us knows, and all of us come with this mixed bag of moments where we are right, and moments we'll be proven wrong, and we can only hope for the best.

But when given the time and chance to look at things from a greater scale, you start to realise the beauty in bending: That things that catch you off guard, things soaked in uncertainty, tend to be the most beautiful.

Consider love, for example: Loving someone for all the absolute facts you know about them, with full certainty of what their weaknesses could be, is fairly easy, if not transactional. But to consider them as an individual with a past and future you don't fully know, with depths you cannot force your way into understanding entirely — to love them in their mystery, is a type of love that is purer, and unconditional.

I just feel like life becomes easier, not harder, when you learn to let go of highly planned outcomes. When you learn to get past the embarrassment, too, or slight bruise to the ego, and admit that you don't and won't know everything, and that your expectations are not source of truth. Because admitting and forgiving yourself for that fault is important too.

And, when you learn to give in, and bite the bullet, and learn to bend without necessarily breaking. The fog just clears. I learn that I don't have to wrestle, because God is not fighting against me, but for me. 

So I sit. And watch the view from that bus, and make room for simply enjoying. I'm very lucky that things turned out differently.
 

 

What's a beauty you found through bending?
