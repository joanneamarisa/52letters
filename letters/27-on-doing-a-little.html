27. On doing a little

by Joanne Amarisa
  
Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 16 December 2022.

My Dear Friend,

I feel like when you haven't done something in a long while, and then you decide to get back to doing it again, what we should feel is proud. Proud for giving ourselves another chance. Brave, for not giving up on us. But instead, what we as a society tends to feel, is shame. Embarrassment for not having gone to the gym in too long, or sense of failure about — *ahem* — not having written any letters in a month's time.

So here I am, writing to you, as a way to combat that self-critical ghoul that tells me I'm a horrible person for not trying hard enough. I want to say November was a whirlwind, and that I've had to make space for other things, which is true, but being forgiving on myself shouldn't require an excuse. We shouldn't need excuses for our shortcomings to determine whether or not we should be nice to ourselves.

So I say this, actually, because it has a little to do with my letter today.

At the train station when I get home from work, I sometimes take the elevator to get from the station's entrance to the train platform downstairs.

It's so silly — literally all you have to do is go down one flight of stairs and you reach the train, and the stairs is how I get to and from the platform everyday.

But there are some days – like this particular day – where I walk to the station with my head down. Mind fuming after work, that faint and drained 5pm feeling we 9-to-5 fighters know all too well. When I'm feeling that bit more... downtrodden (for lack of a less ancient word), my inner critic is far less friendly, and that commute home feels a bit more challenging. I feel like I just want to cocoon and hide and just be.

Hence, I once in a while find myself standing on that empty elevator for 3-4 seconds, coming down one floor to take me to a platform I could've easily reached on my own.

Why? Because I can. Because no reason at all. Because I'm feeling like I need a hug or a bed and I couldn't get to that just yet, so the comforting convenience of an elevator will have to do.

Shortly after, I came across a TED Talk titled How to do laundry when you're depressed. In it, KC Davis proposes a simple, yet profound connection between cleaning tasks and mental health. Because isn't it true that when we're dipped in grief, burnout, stress, or anxiety, we can forget what it's like to vacuum our floors or make our bed? Aren't there days when the little things are not so little?

"Care tasks," she mentioned, "like cooking and cleaning, are morally neutral. They don't make you a good person, or a bad person. An organised closet doesn't make you a success. A pile of laundry on the floor doesn't make you a failure."

How long it has been since you last cleaned your bedroom, doesn't make you more or less worthy of a functional space. How long it has been since you last did your laundry, or cleaned the dishes, makes you no more or less worthy, than the ability to eat, and to sleep in clean sheets.

Later that week, I went out with a few friends of mine to grab ice cream with one of our former lecturers. We sat at the park, and our lecturer was telling us about her PhD research on food waste. My friend then said, "That's so true! Young couples probably waste food a lot. My partner and I, we call them jank meals — you basically throw anything you find from the fridge into a meal. And that's a jank meal. But it works."

It works. Because when you can't cook, you put fried rice into a wrap or you pair a half-sandwich with a packet of chips.

Because when you can't take a shower, "you take a packet of wet wipes," Davis says in her TED talk, "because no matter how you're feeling, you still deserve to be clean."

Go for the unorthodox ways of taking care of yourself if that means still taking care of yourself, whatever that may look like.

If I can't find it in me to run whole laps today, I'm going to have to make peace with my yoga mat sprawled on the lawn outside. (I'll sit and stretch or something, I don't know?) If I can't bring myself to change my bed sheets, I'll settle on just my pillowcase or I'll grab a different blanket.

"Give yourself permission to do a little." So here I am, friends. Doing a little. Doing what I can.

Writing to you.
 

What's the "little" that you can do this week?
