45. I sailed

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 18 August 2023.

My Dear Friend,

I was running late.

I admit, I am chronically tardy, and constantly underestimate the time it takes me to get from point A to point B.

But I swear, I can explain this time.

Minutes before my doctor's appointment (aforementioned point B), I did pace around my tram stop and thought, "Okay, if I catch this next one, I'll just be three minutes late past the appointment time. I'm sure that's fine!"

The tram was eight minutes away. What I like to do when this happens – being someone who ironically is always late, but hates waiting still in one place – is I walk my way to the next tram stop, only a few hundred metres down the road, just to pass the time.

So on I walked, past a souvlaki place and a basketball court and a kids' playground and a grooming salon, until I reached the second stop and waited for the tram to arrive. I could see the previous stop from where I was standing, so could also see when the tram did eventually appear. 

Perfect, I thought, only a minute 'til it moves my way.

Only, it didn't. The tram halted, in fact, at that previous stop—the one I'd just left. 

What's going on? It was further up the road, and I could hardly see the driver nor any passenger. The tram stayed where it was for five long, agonising minutes. Soon, I spotted passengers alighting the tram, making their way, on foot, towards my direction. 

Oh no, I thought. This tram literally broke down.

I panicked. I only had about 10 minutes to make it to a clinic that was 4 kilometres away. I could either shell out for a $20 Uber ride – and even if the Uber came with a wait time, I would've been late either way. I couldn't see nor hear what was going on in that tram given I'd walked this far up the road away from where it halted. I had no other way of getting to the clinic, no officers to ask, no station in sight.

And that's when I saw it. A lime-green Lime bike perched near the entrance to a housing complex.

I mean, I could...? 

I looked around. Oh God, this would be embarrassing. But also, how fun would that be??

I approached the bike. Should I?? I can ride one. I haven't in at least a whole year. What if a car honks at me? What if something happens? What if I miss a turn?

Do I have a choice? I looked back at the tram, still unmoved. It seems I do not.

In a movie, I'd imagine this act would be accompanied by a rock-and-roll soundtrack. This isn't a movie, so I'm going to need you, Friend, to imagine it as I narrate this story, to make it seem cooler than it is.

Still in panic mode, I frantically scanned the QR code, figuring out how a Lime bike actually works, while pulling up my Google Maps at the same time, and putting on a dusty helmet and peeking into the front basket that, I'm pretty sure, have been well used by the locals as a public ashtray.

Nice.

I slung my bag over my shoulder. Welp, here goes nothing. With no time to lose, saddled over the seat only to realise this was my first time ever on an e-bike.

I wobbled awkwardly off the curb, onto the road, made my first pedal only to almost topple onto a parked car.

Damn it, Jo. Get it together.

I tried again, and once I got it—the acceleration and weight that goes with a motorised bike—I finally pedalled.

Cobwebs peeled off the tyres as I propelled forward for the first time. (I could literally hear a crunching sound, like I had cracked this vehicle awake.)

And I pedalled left, and right, and left, and right, until I got the hang of it, and it was like I could do it forever.

When I tell you, Friend, I sailed. This god-awful neon green long-forgotten bike with cigarettes in her basket and I.

I lunged forward and sailed up and down the hilly Malvern Road, peering at passing houses and barren trees and swerving smoothly past construction sites, courteously signalling for cars to let me pass.

It felt like I could do anything. Frosty wind and sunlight both pressed against my face and cheeks, and I smiled. I might as well have been in the open sea.

I felt so free, so childlike. Also so crazy, exhilarated, and so out of my mind.

Yet like the cobwebs, my crusted fears fell off that bike.

Imagine not doing this? I thought. What's stopping me? What would have?

Minutes later, I sat at the waiting room patting down my messy hair, slightly out of breath, still smiling.

Have you ever thought about what's stopping us, Dear Friend?

What's stopping you?
