39. Early bird gets the poem

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 11 May 2023.

My Dear Friend,

I signed up for a poetry class for the first time ever recently. Even if it fell on 4am Melbourne time and I have never taken a single poetry class in my life. I haven't written a poem in years.

The class is taught by Philip Kaye, an author and poet who have introduced and shared to many around the world (including me) the craft of spoken word poetry.

Before workshops began, he emailed me to ask if I can share a few sentences about myself and my path in writing. I wrote, "I'd like to explore, with poetry, how to be braver."

Writing is the thing that is dearest to my heart. So I thrive in writing classes, and I don't mean thrive as in succeed, but thrive as in it's the most myself I ever feel. People I connect with through writing have formed the deepest connections I've experienced. It's hard to study writing without also studying the self.

So there I was yesterday, reading a poem out loud to a classmate in a Zoom Breakout room. The 2-3 hours of sleep time I sacrificed didn't waver my excitement. In fact, this meant that for the next two Wednesdays (which is not enough Wednesdays!) I would end up with one written poem by the time the world around me wakes.

Early dawn quickly becomes my favourite time of day. The sky brightens just a touch for me to see the outlines of buildings, but not enough to perceive colour. The temperature is the coldest it will be on that day, and I'm kept in warm company by silence, a lamp, and all these words, floating off the page.

A selah. Like Romeo & Juliet, sneaking off at night into the gardens—my kept little secret.

I wrestle with courage and confidence as these are things I cannot forecast. Things that ebb and flow and rise and fall as quickly as my own breath. We stretch our right foot in, sometimes leaving our left foot out, and pray that something wonderful happens. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't, or it does but not without the hard parts. All I know is suddenly, I am reading poetry to a (Zoom) room full of strangers.

A couple of hours later, the world will wake. And I will get swept once again into its noise. But for now, I let myself morph into the poet I don't think I am. And on I write. I have all the time in the world to write.
 
 
What's one way you find solace?

A poem:
"Surplus" by Philip Kaye
