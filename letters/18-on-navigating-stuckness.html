18. On navigating stuckness

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 24 August 2022.

Dear Friend,

A few weeks ago, I remember feeling... stuck. Not in the literal sense, but in the sense that life likes to take its time, while my mind wants to go at full speed ahead. 

Life unfolds at its own pace, as we're all aware, whereas we as humans are programmed to desire, living in a culture that tells us there's always more that we can have or become. This tug of war brings about an anxious restlessness in many — including myself — where we feel we're doing too much but not enough. More familiarly, this would sound something like: I should be ______ by now.

When I feel stuck, there is usually an urge to numb out, to look at what other people are doing, to hide... So instead, three things I am willing to practice in this coming month:

Paying closer attention.

I normally want to disengage in seasons of stuckness, which are really seasons of discomfort and maybe boredom. But it is precisely in feelings of stuckness that indicate I need to be paying closer attention. 

When I feel stuck at my job, for instance, it's easy to disengage and spend months just going through the motions. (It's very easy to spend a lot of time going through the motions!) The flaw is it makes us lose sight of where we are.

You feel stuck because you're dissatisfied, dissatisfaction leads to disengagement, disengaging leads to being less present and when you're not present, you picture a different life where you are seemingly more "ahead". Nothing wrong with it of course, but when you constantly want it and draw comparisons with your present life, you feel stuck, and on goes the cycle.

So to counter it, I pay closer attention. I try to think of building better habits on my commute, try to actually turn my chair and join conversations at work I normally would've opted out of, try to listen more intently and really drive my ego to the ground to figure out what skills or traits I lack. Rediscovering is a remedy to stuckness, and most of the time — once I let go of the pride that says "I've seen it all" — I'm pleasantly surprised by what I find.

Avoid over-identifying.

There is a finiteness attached to stuckness — when I feel stuck I feel like this is all I'm getting. This is as good as it'll get. There's no way I can break out of this. They won't accept me. I won't be able to get to where I want. My circumstances make my aspirations impossible.

In self help books, this is called the 'fixed mindset'. They are limiting beliefs that you are where you are because it's "impossible" to progress. This is something I've been trying to divorce from within myself, too, which is done by seriously reassessing the stories I tell myself.

It's important to distance our identity and self worth from the seasons we're in. If I were to identify with my failures, for example, I'd have a very hard time not feeling stuck, because then I would think I don't have what it takes or I'm not cut out for it. Fixed mindsets close the door to future improvements and possibilities.

So I'm trying to adopt more growth mindset-fuelled stories: Now I know what my capacity is, at this point in time. My capabilities can expand or shrink over time. I'm allowed to prioritise. I'm allowed to make adjustments on where I'm going, based on the information I have. Not being a fit for one thing doesn't mean I don't have a place in this world.

Now in general, I don't believe any effort is fruitless. I don't believe any time is wasted and I don't believe failures or rejections or things-not-going-my-way are prophecies of doom. I don't deny that they hurt, and I don't deny that my own shortcomings play a role in these scenarios — I've just decided that they aren't reflective of my value and worth as a person.

Reject the idea that being satisfied is equal to settling for less. 

My first ever therapist said to me once: "You seem to have the urge to want to 'leap' to this better place from where you are now. It's admirable, however the real changes only happen once you are okay with where you are. Being okay with yourself comes first."

Being okay with yourself comes first.

However way you're feeling or whichever constraint you feel stuck in (be it a job or an identity or a relationship status), I think it's important to take escapism out of the equation. It is true that being dissatisfied is often a catalyst that drives people into action (including good action!) But until we address the arbitrary metrics we use to measure our own success or progress in life, then stuckness & dissatisfaction will follow us wherever we go.

Something I say to God when I find myself desiring for something (too) strongly is, "God, help me trust, and teach me to steward the soil." It basically means: Don't put me anywhere else if I'm not stewarding, or taking care of, my own 'soil'. David tended to sheep. What is my soil? What have I been given? How much? Am I handling it well?

The question shouldn't be: God, where can you take me? The bigger question should be: From where I'm standing now, am I yielding good, lasting harvest?

There are always people to love, habits to build, limiting beliefs to shed, and dreams to gracefully and patiently work towards. I believe none of us are ever as stuck — not at 21, not at 53 — as we are in need of the reminder to pay deeper attention, learn to make peace with where we are, and solidify (or broaden) our understanding of ourselves.

So what about you? How do you get un-stuck?
