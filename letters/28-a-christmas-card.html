28. A Christmas card

by Joanne Amarisa
Jakarta, Indonesia. Monday, 26 December 2022.

My Dearest Friend,

On universally momentous days like Christmas, I tend to turn into the human embodiment of Inside Out's Sadness sometimes. While I love the Home Alone marathons and Mariah Carey sing-alongs (and, *cough* spending too much of my savings on Christmas gift shopping), there are moments where I also become this blue little thing, feeling melancholy for the people for whom holidays like these can be more difficult.

When the whole world is in celebration, any tinge of sorrow can feel very alien. So it feels very awkward, very out-of-place to feel anything but "jolly", when in reality, it's very possible to not feel your best on December 25th.

A few weeks ago I was reading a letter from Mari Andrew. In it, she writes that the language of gratitude — "I'm grateful I have a job! I'm thankful for my marriage!" — can make us dismissive of the hard stuff — "I'm actually feeling stuck... We're actually going through a rough patch..."

So where do those feelings go?

I was hoping here. In a simple Christmas card. In a flimsy envelope that I hope can hold all the holiday feelings that have nowhere to go.

Feelings like loneliness, even when seated around a dinner table. Feelings like sadness, because maybe you're homesick or it's your first Christmas without a loved one. Feelings like fear, because maybe you're anxious about the new year. Feelings like fatigue, because mental health struggles or heartaches or financial worries don't vanish overnight — even on a day like Christmas.

And what even is a day like Christmas? What must it look like? How should it feel?

Because I've cried on Christmases before. I've smiled or laughed too. I've felt loved, I've felt lost, and every year a bit of both is true. The older I get the less I expect the season to be flawlessly cheery. I realise Christmases can be messy, can be a bit devastating for different reasons, can bring out the best or worst in us, just like any other day in the year. No matter where we are and who we spend it with, Decembers can always invite a mixture of joy and gratitude and fear and 'lost'-ness.

And they all deserve space. They all deserve a seat at the table. In fact, it's important that they meet each other: For the fear to meet the hope. For the love to meet the loneliness. 

"The howling accompanies the hope;" as Mari Andrew wrote. "The gratitude is friends with the grieving."

So I suppose, have a Christmas. (If you don't feel like a "merry" one this year.)

Have a week, have a New Year's Eve. Look at fireworks, or don't! That's cool! Spend it by yourself, spend it with your loved ones, spend it sleeping, treat it like a normal Saturday night. Go out and celebrate and make goals. Or treat it like the last week of the calendar and little else. Spend this holiday season in however way that makes sense to you. The "jollyness" will be here next year.

I wish you joy & comfort. And not an elusive wish of "joy & comfort will come to you somehow someday" — but wishes that begin today. Right now, I wish comfort finds you. Like a comfort film, an activity, a decision big or small. Any blanket or hug or both, that can make you breathe a little easier from this very minute. I wish you that this Christmas.

I don't have a film to recommend to you — I'm kidding. I have 200 of course. But hey, Inside Out is a very cute one to start with. I think back to its main message on holidays or get-togethers like these: With the help of sadness, we feel the most joyful. With the familiarity of loneliness, we know to cherish and value togetherness.

And lastly, I believe you. I believe in you, yes, but I also believe you — especially if no one has told you this lately. I believe you if you say this is the happiest Christmas you've had, I believe you if you say it's your saddest. I believe your gratitude, I believe your goals. I believe whatever you desire or want for the new year, doesn't make you 'ungrateful'. Whatever you say you need, I believe you. However way you choose to describe this year, I believe you.

And with that, your little blue friend signs off for the night (and the year, perhaps), hoping her blue-tainted Christmas card, one day overdue, will do.

Have a restful week, Friend. I'll write to you again soon.
