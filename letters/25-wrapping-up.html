25. Wrapping up

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Sunday, 6 November 2022.
As written on my journal. 8:53 AM, Dog Park.

My Dear Friend,

At precisely 8:30 AM on days when the sky is particularly clear, sunlight falls on the four benches that occupy the sunny side of the Victoria Gardens dog park.

This is where I would normally sit on Sunday mornings. But today, all of the spots were occupied.

Not fully, though. And I was determined to sit somewhere soaked in sun, on a perfectly sunny day like this. So I gripped my coffee a little tighter, and mustered up the courage to say to a stranger, "Excuse me, do you mind if I sit here?"

No, not at all! the young woman replied. So that's how I found myself sat next to her at 8:45, as she plays fetch with her Dachshund-Jack Russell mix, Albi.

They have left now. Albi barks a lot when he doesn't get his ball, so when fetch time was over, he was unrelenting. The woman calmly decided they'll go for a walk instead.

"Well, enjoy!" she said to me. "Are you journaling? Is that what you're doing? Oh – such a nice day for it!"

I don't know if every stranger sitting on a bench would be as welcoming. I don't know if other parts of the world would even have nice parks and nice strangers and barky dogs who want their ball back.

So I savour this.

The way I savour everything to a sickeningly romantic state nowadays. I take the train and attempt to mentally capture everything. I treat mundaneness with indulgence. I really snuggle in on rainy days and really take 2-hour walks when the skies are blue. I chew slowly and try to be a better listener and not feel so guilty for spending an hour doing nothing but reading a book.

Everything is so finite and fleeting. I mean, it's almost Christmas. If we blink, we might miss it. 

October felt riotous, so I'm very relieved we've come up for air. While the end-of-year stress at work hasn't really subsided, but I take my romanticised train ride home every night and think, "Today was enough."

November offers a graceful reminder to finally let the year go. Lessons about wrapping up and letting go are really lessons about acceptance of what is left, and trusting in what's ahead. When the Christmas tunes start to play and the tinsel begins appearing in supermarkets or window stalls, we are reminded that it's time to wrap the year up.

I'm not great at wrapping things up. I know because I've spent one too many evenings working overtime, or too many nights staying up late doing tasks I swore I'd do that day. I'm also bad at considering a piece — be it in writing or art — as "finished". I always want to edit and perfect to the point of oblivion everything I put out into the world.

It's unsustainable and exhausting. And that's why I gladly succumb to the ringing bells of Christmas time. When baubled pine trees appear, it's as if they say, Ready or not, let's wrap the year up. Do what you have to, but let go of what was. Everything you've done has been enough. Everything you haven't done is forgiven.

Because you are still here, and because there is a new year waiting for all of us. So does it even matter how perfect we all become in the end? Is not being in the present already worth the celebration?

The dogs are waddling home now. I should probably leave too.

Even after I say that, I'm still fiddling with my pen, thinking whether I have anything more to say.

I don't, though. Not for today.

I hope you have a nice November, friend. Be kind to yourself, ok?

Sincerely,
Jo
