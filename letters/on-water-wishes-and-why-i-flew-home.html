On water, wishes, and why I flew home

by Joanne Amarisa
Jakarta, Indonesia. Sunday, 12 June 2022.

My Dear Friend,

The last time I spent my birthday at home in Indonesia, I was turning 18. It was five years ago; I was also in Bali, on a farewell trip with some of my high school friends, and the memory of that morning is still as vivid to me as ever.

I woke up in the dark, silent cavern that was our hotel room at 7 AM, next to 3-4 friends who were still asleep. I didn't touch my phone, and instead reached for a bathing suit, tiptoed out of the room, down the stairs, and into the pool, where I spent the next hour in a blissful daze.

How fortunate am I to return to the same island, five years later. On the morning of my 23rd birthday, I walked along a glossy beach in Seminyak, as close as I can get to the ocean. I'm not sure what it is about spending birthdays near bodies of water — how healing and euphoric it feels to be reeled in by a different gravity, on a day where milestones and memories take on a more colossal weight. I think I'd like to keep the tradition.

That morning, I let the waves drench the bottom half of my shorts, leaving my legs with dots of sand and saltwater. I think of where on earth I'll be next year. I think of the contrast of being 18 versus 23, as illustrated by the water that welcomes me — the pool, this calm, collected, controlled little thing; versus the ocean, a gargantuan beast of a character, unpredictable, and unashamed.
 
...
 
Gravity is heavier on land. Only three days later, I am on the phone with a friend, sobbing. "Why am I like this?" I whined, and at this point I was more laughing at myself.

It was a Monday night. My brother had been in town that weekend, and Monday was going to be his last night staying with us. I hadn't seen him in years prior, and I've been saying to myself I'm going to spend as much time with him as I can before his wedding day. 

That Monday, though, I don't know if it was the onslaught of cross-continental working, or just the typical plow of a Monday, but I mis-planned. I didn't finish work in time to tag along with him to run some wedding errands, which I told him I wanted to do since the day before. I forgot to set myself a reminder; I looked at the clock and I was 30 minutes late. My brother had to leave, I felt guilty, and my brother, laidback and big-hearted, thought nothing of it and said, "It's fine! I'll see you at home tonight."

Somehow I felt even worse. Thirty minutes after my brother's car pulled out of the driveway, my guilt was in overdrive. If I spent all that time in Melbourne wishing I was here, and now that I'm here I fail to make time for my loved ones, then what am I? I thought.

I decided to reach out to a friend I can talk to. Shame festers and grows when it's hidden, or at least that's what Brené Brown's book taught me. When you feel ashamed, speaking about it out loud helps. Including helping me see how unreasonably hard I was being on myself.

It wasn't the error of timing that struck me. It was how I promised to do one thing, and failed to deliver. I said I was going to do something, and didn't do it. Yet this is described perfectly in her book, Gifts of Imperfection, where Brené writes this:
 
"We feel guilty when we hold up something we've done or failed to do against the kind of person we want to be. It's an uncomfortable feeling, but one that's helpful. When we apologise for something we've done, make amends to others, or change a behaviour that we don't feel good about, guilt is most often the motivator."

For the record, it turned out fine; I went for dinner with friends, came back home, and apologised to my brother when I saw him. He genuinely didn't mind, and was even unfazed for the most part. The next day we were already sprawled across the dining table with our laptops open again, drinking iced coffee while he and his fiancé ask me which colour looks better on the invitation. 

I'm yet to learn to be more compassionate of my own blunders. And if I myself were one of the people I told that night, she'd say something like: "That actually sounds very human." (And I'd hate that I'm right.)

I share this with you not because I am surprised of my error (quite the contrary.) But I'd like to lend you this thought to pocket when you do reach milestones or life events, such as turning a new age:

The person you want to become, makes mistakes too.

This applies to whether that person is a CEO or simply a better sister. I sense that we struggle with the archetypal: I thought I'd already be ........... by now. And depending on your stage of life, this can be: I thought I'd be (manager / CEO / owning a home / getting a master's degree / a better sister / a better friend / living elsewhere / in a relationship / having children) by now.

But ask anyone's journey of home-buying and they'll tell you what they wish they knew sooner. Ask a seemingly perfect mother and she has a laundry list of all the things she's done horribly wrong. The flaw with believing that our life will be "on track" once we reach a certain point, is we dismiss the reality that all our annoying human-ness will continue to follow us. No life change renders you flawless. I can be 18, 23, 45, and I still won't fall short of life lessons I learn the hard way.

One of those lessons that continues to follow me is this: I cannot be everything to everyone.

The events of Monday night led me to realise how I've been struggling with balance in this past 3 weeks. I am not used to having to balance work, my own personal life, and family; it has always been mainly 2 of the 3. 

Prioritising family is much easier to do while living away from them, which will sound a bit like a cheat. It's manageable by way of video calls, photo updates, the perpetual group chat... But choosing to prioritise family while living with them requires me to be more accountable. This includes better planning, better making-of-time rather than giving them my "free time", and lots and lots of patience (my 4-year-old niece looooves to barge in when I'm just about to start a Zoom meeting.) Did I mention loss of personal space, or sacred "quiet" time? Yeah, I've kissed those things goodbye.

So the lesson, in a more complete sentence might be: I cannot be anything to anyone if I am nothing to myself.

Our cup has to be replenished, they say, before we can pour to another's. My decision to fly back home, in case you were wondering, stemmed from this thought. It is the most radical doing-this-for-myself thing I think I have ever done. A dramatic uprooting from a scenic, happy life in Melbourne, yet I knew that if I didn't do it, if I even discounted the desire, I'd continue to live there with no ability left to be anything to anyone.

And I've never felt this happy. It is Saturday in sunny Jakarta. We're taking my brother to a tailor's appointment tomorrow to see him fitted in his wedding suit. The past three weeks there are moments where I catch myself smiling stupidly, in simple disbelief that I can feel so at home.

I cannot be a perfect sister, daughter, aunt, friend, co-worker, employee, all at once. What I can do is apologise for mistakes I would inevitably make, be honest with everyone and myself, ask for help or support when I need it, and practice to receive love and forgiveness on days when I feel least deserving of it.

On the Monday night where my guilt was in overdrive, the friend I called over the phone listened to me and knew me well enough to respond to my distress. "You know I'm not great at advice," she said, "but maybe go take a shower first. I don't know why, but water always seems to help."

She was right. It does.
