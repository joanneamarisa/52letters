35. My theme of the year

by Joanne Amarisa

Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 31 March 2023.

My Dearest Friend,

As I was walking home with a bag of groceries tonight, with Ella Fitzgerald singing in my earpods, I thought about what I should write about in my next letter. So I got home, stashed my groceries away, sat down and opened TinyLetter, scrolled through the drafts, and found this one.

It was marked as letter 32, but sometimes another topic or story becomes more vivid so I write about that other thing instead, and stow some letters away for another day. I read through this one again, and today felt like that other day. So here you go — a letter from the past, written early February 2023. Enjoy.
 

January is the sibling that makes us late to walk out the door. She never rushes – January stretches lazily after she wakes, fumbles to the bathroom, and takes her precious time picking an outfit and tying her shoelaces. She ignores our frantic annoyance: "Hurry up!! We have to go!" Lounges on the couch and chews her breakfast slowly, while we're gathering our things, pacing the home, anxious for the day to begin.

That's how long and arduous it felt. So much so that I repeatedly thought, Oh my gosh, you're only a month?

I am more used to the rush. When something like a new year or season dawns upon us, our minds become fretful because the uncertainty taunts us, the plans excite us, and everything else in-between seems so up to chance.

So instead of subjecting myself to this kind of energy at the start of each new year, I am wondering if there is a better way.

I'm writing this to you from a half-empty apartment. I have a mattress but no bed frame, a sofa but not yet assembled. I'm sitting on a nice balcony chair, which I waddled & carried across the lobby myself, and I have a well-stocked little bookshelf and a friend's drawing on my bedside table. A telling snapshot of what I consider as bare necessities?

For the past month a few friends have asked me about my annual "theme" – one I always enjoy coming up with every year. From 2019-2022, my range of themes included: Bravery, Apologising Less, Do & See What Happens.

When I was writing my last letter to you, I had a semblance of what this year's was, but not yet a word or phrase that felt fitting. My theme of the year felt more suitable manifested as a physical gesture, rather than a written phrase. And those gestures would be: holding a locket to your chest; drifting off to sleep; a self-hug; putting one hand on your chest, pressing gently, and taking deeeeep deep breaths.

My theme of the year is comfort. It's ease. It's making myself into a home, a place where we switch off our survival mode – and we are safe, and kept, and loved, and worthy of a chance, no matter what happens.

A theme isn't as straightforward as a resolution. I didn't have a step 1, step 2, step 3. Rather, they rely on constant reminders – so here are 3 of mine:

1. To fight with myself less
It's not easy to switch off survival mode. We are programmed to hustle and be productive and to always be ahead, whatever that means. Even my colour-coordinated Google Calendar, despite me being proud of it, can slowly but surely consume my life if I'm not careful.

But if success came from demanding more of myself, of seeing how close we can get to burnout — if that works as well as we think it would, then all of us would be swimming in college degrees and job offers and accolades. Instead, I'm just sitting here in my lounge chair, watching the sky turn purple. (And it's beautiful, I'm telling you!)

At age 23, I'm learning what it means to live at a more forgiving pace. Fighting with myself less over what I have to do in a given week or day. Setting more realistic deadlines for goals — for example, not telling myself to finish an online course in 2 nights, or publish 3 videos in one day. Being more patient and accepting that the life I want will most likely take a long time to build. So I should enjoy the time to build.

2. To draw from a deep well
Between resigning from my job, moving apartments, amongst other personal life changes, I'd say that this year has been A Lot. (And I say this on... Day 38?)

There are podcasts and interviews and books ready to give me advice on navigating these changes, but my favourite has to be a tweet by Rabbi Danya Ruttenberg, written on June 2020 at the height of COVID-19:
A deep, deep part of the well. From the pandemic, it became clear that what saves us amidst adversity or change, is a constant. That constant can be a prayer, an act, a person.

For me, it's the first few steps of a morning walk. The warm light on my exhaust fan, and the sizzling sounds of cooking dinner. It's that silent 1-2 minutes leaning on the kitchen counter, waiting for my kettle to boil. It's my devotionals, my embarrasingly long journal pages, my prayers to God comprised of sentences I myself am still not sure I understand. I also have two vials of skincare products that I swear are doing something – I'm just not sure what.

I call these my "deep parts of the well" – the stuff we don't always make time to access, or are even guilty about indulging in because they're not conventionally productive. Yes, they are small, sometimes even mundane things, but that doesn't make them trivial. In fact, sometimes they're necessary to survival. 

In storms, you're not praying for the grandeur of a ship. You praise heavens for the simplest, most functional form of a life raft.

3. To let people in
[This part, I added and wrote in today.]

When we are together, my friends and I easily slide into topics of psychology: our personalities, communication styles, relationships, habits. You can say we're a very safe, self-aware bunch (if not just weirdly addicted to psychoanalysing ourselves voluntarily.) But we prefer the term "comfortable with each other"!

Anyway, one of the conversations we had a few weeks ago was around strengths and weaknesses. In a group of 4, each of us took turns in naming one weakness our friend had, followed by one strength. I wouldn't tell you what the group said was mine, of course, but I could tell you one of the major lessons I got from that conversation: to let people see you, and to let people in.

There are two myths that have hindered this for me. First, up until recently, I used to think letting people in meant signing a pledge to share about everything to everyone. But Brené Brown writes in her book that there is a difference between vulnerability and oversharing.

“I call it floodlighting," Brené says. "A lot of times we share too much information as a way to protect us from vulnerability. [...] Oversharing is not vulnerability. Vulnerability requires boundaries, and they’re different for everyone. Don’t share anything where your self-worth or healing depends upon the reaction you get back." (Full interview here.)

That's something I've learned only in the past year, but I'm glad I did. Part of applying that is to not share about, or write about, things we haven't processed internally. (Which I used to do a lot, and instead of helping me heal, this would lead me to stress more, and worse, compromised my sense of safety or made me feel "exposed".) 

I'm learning to better recognise when sharing helps and when sharing doesn't. For example, one month after going through a significant life change, I was sitting on a train with a friend, and she asked, "How have you been? Is everything okay?"

And I did something I don't think I'd ever done before. I told her the truth, without crossing the boundary of what I felt comfortable to share, but also without sugarcoating my experience. 

I answered, "I'm not, actually. But thanks so much for asking. Just a lot of stressful stuff recently, but I'll probably tell you once I've had time to process it myself. I don't feel ready to talk about it at all."

She responded lovingly, "Aw Jo, I'm sorry. I'm here if you want to talk about it. If you need anything at all!" and I said, "Thank you so much. I really appreciate it." And that was it. Which brings me to my second myth: That people don't care, or that we somehow become "burdens" by sharing.

Each time, I've always found the opposite to be proven true. Genuine friendships get strengthened by vulnerability, not threatened. In fact, as a friend, I'd be more hurt if I knew my friend refused to let me be there for them, versus if they had come to me because they needed a listening ear (in which I'd feel glad that they trusted me, and glad to offer anything, even if it were just minutes of my time.)

So perhaps — perhaps — people do have space for us. Not limitless space, but space regardless. Those boundaries vary case by case, but comfort and ease happen when we insist less on fending for ourselves, on our own. Even primally, we are created and designed to need each other.

So need.
