49. My word of the year

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Sunday, 18 February 2024.
My Dearest Friend,

Last night, I was part of the chorus of 90,000 Swifties belting out the words to Long Live, shaking the insides of Melbourne Cricket Ground.

As the way I often feel when listening to any of Taylor's older records, standing there in a crowd of light-up bracelets and flawlessly bedazzled people, I felt waves of nostalgia.

Though I love folklore and Lover and her newer albums, it's always tracks from Speak Now, Fearless, or her debut, that always gets to me. It takes me right back to sitting in the car on my way to school, or my way home from school, as we decked out the dashboard with all of her CDs. (I would be flipping through her CD cover booklets to look at the photos and lyrics—remember those??)

Nostalgia is something I've been thinking a lot about lately. There was a time during the Christmas holidays when I was scrolling through the photos I took in 2023—as one does as you try to process the passing year.

Row to row, variations of myself smiled back at me. It is February, and I am beaming after successfully assembling a couch for the first time. It's May, and my friend and I are sitting across each other, laughing, sipping wine after watching a show. It's November, and I am reading a book at a beach and that was probably the only time in the entire year that I read a book at a beach.

Many songs and poems lament about "missing our old selves", and sometimes I do feel that, sentimental as it may be. Yet if I were to step back into February, May, or November Jo's shoes—would she had been thinking that? Did she notice, at that point, as she was sitting with that book on the beach, that she would go a few months down the road and miss that moment she was living?

Did she remember to take it all in, and savour every bit?

...

"You may believe that living life to the fullest is seeing every country in the world and quitting your job on a whim and falling recklessly in love, but it's really just knowing how to be where your feet are.

It's learning how to take care of yourself, how to make a home within your own skin. How to build a simple life you are proud of. A life most fully lived is not always composed of the things that rock you awake, but those that slowly assure you it's okay to slow down. That you don't always have to prove yourself. That you don't need to fight forever, or constantly want more. That it's okay for things to be just as they are.

Little by little, you will begin to see that life can only grow outward in proportion to how stable it is inward—that if the joy is not in the little things first, the big things won't fully find us."

–Brianna Wiest

...

A lot of 2023 was good. A lot of 2023 was also hard. Yet overarchingly, I can admit that I spent a lot of 2023 trying to solve or "get through" the hard stuff, often forgetting to be grateful for the good stuff.

And we can live the rest of our lives like this, if we are not careful. We often think that missing out on life is not landing that job, not getting to go on that trip, not going to that bar with those friends, not reaching the goals we've set for ourselves to feel like we "made it".

Now entering my mid-twenties, I have a different view on what missing out really is. It's failing to notice that there's always something to be grateful for. That life is good—not always perfect, neither will we make perfect decisions, but life's always at least a little good in each passing minute—and if we can't see it today, our nostalgic future selves will see it crystal clearly. 

When I ask a person who is a few years older than me for advice, they may tell me to do A, B, and C to get to here or there. Yet when I ask people much older than me for advice—and this has included Uber drivers, to relatives, to my door repairman—they always say something along the lines of: My God, just enjoy it. Don't worry too much.

That's why my word of the year—though I am not sure whether it is a word—is presentness. 

It's the idea that the happiness we all dream of and long for ourselves, doesn't have to exist only in moments long gone, or in moments to come. That we can find joy in the here and now, and be grateful with even a little—be grateful even while trying to make peace with what is difficult.

We're so programmed to constantly be thinking about "our next move" nowadays, especially when you're in your twenties. High school Jo was probably also thinking or daydreaming about her next move, while casually listening to Taylor in the back seat of her car. Yet here she is, 5-10 years older, and all she reminisces about is the simplest routine of being in that car, and knowing which song in the CD came next.

So I want to learn to stop wishing away the season I'm in. It's less about finding the remnant of the past that you can summon into your present, neither is it about finding the one perfect 5-year roadmap to what will happen in your life and when. 

Rather, it's about learning to bask as much, and as well, as you can in the present moment. When you learn to be where you feet are. When you stop looking at the present as transitional, because the seasons you're dying to get out of now, will most likely someday be a season you miss.

You can give many moments your presence, and not your presentness. You can be sitting in a train wishing your commutes didn't exist. You can be sitting at your day job looking for an exit strategy. You can be sitting at a dinner table with your family and not really listening.

Or, you can sit in that train and listen to an amazing podcast, or read a Kindle (one of the things I plan to try this year!) You can be sitting at your day job and be fuelled by that great cup of coffee. You can be sitting with your family and really listen when your parents tell you that story for the 57th time.

So I don't know, wear that perfume and lace up those shoes maybe. Go out and as you live your life, spend less time wishing you were elsewhere. Consider, before strategising your next big life change, if you could first make your perspective change and make your heart be at peace with where it is. Consider that maybe that should come first.

You might've heard the phrase: There's no way around this except through. Grit is often what they mean by "the way through". But really, gratitude is a way through it too.

Capture it, remember it.
