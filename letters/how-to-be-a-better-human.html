How to be a better human

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 12 May 2022.

My Dear Friend,

On days where I feel exceptionally inspired to self-improve, I listen to this TED podcast called How to be a Better Human. 

I often need it because it's hard to human sometimes. In this new, post-pandemic world, there are days where I don't feel quite like myself. Do you feel that way too? My pastor said earlier last week: Whether we like it or not, we are simply not the same people that we were before the pandemic. It's hard to deny this even as we move through the world; there's a sense of fatigue and collective overwhelm even as we settle back to happy normalcy.

One morning on my walk — we're still in freezing autumn— I listened to an episode called How to nurture your emotional agility. I didn't know what that meant, but agility sounded fascinating; like a dog show where they jump through hoops and fences or something.

The speaker is Susan David, psychologist and author of the book Emotional Agility: Get Unstuck, Embrace Change and Thrive in Work and Life. "Emotional agility," she describes, "is the ability to be with our thoughts, emotions and stories in ways that use these as important pieces of data about the things we care about." It's phrased as agility because this process enables us to navigate life's many twists and turns. (I'd say a pandemic can count as a Strong Twist and Turn.)

It's timely, because I've grown tired of the abstract concepts we use to talk about emotions. I can describe them all day long and not run out of metaphors; clouds, leaves, little gremlins, what have you. But post-pandemic pragmatic Jo demands, Do you have something to teach me or not? And as it turns out, they do.

Exercise 1: Signposts

First, reflect on these past 24 months, from the beginning of the pandemic until today. If you were to take a piece of paper and write down one word that describes the emotion you've been feeling (or what you feel now) in this time, what would that be?

The word might be grief, loneliness, boredom, contentment, wonder... And afterwards, turn your paper over and consider: What needs and values is this word signalling to? For example, if your word is boredom, you might be someone who values growth. If it's loneliness, you might be someone who needs connection or genuine intimacy, and is not experiencing much of it in this time. Emotions signpost to whether or not our needs are being met, and whether or not our values are reflected in our current experiences.

My word was languished, which surprised me that it was the first word that came to mind, since it's a relatively new term that I don't even use in my everyday vocabulary. The synonyms for it are stagnation or purposelessness. And this signposts that I'm someone who values purpose — abstract as that word may be.

One of the hard things I've had to reckon with in my 'languishing' this year was admitting that, at one point, I was unhappy. Life can be beautiful filled with things and people you're grateful for, and yet you may still feel a longing to the point of sorrow. In my case, it was starting to show. Writing a feeling down on a piece of paper may seem trite, but this reckoning is important in deciding where we go next. 

Exercise 2: Micro actions

The word "values" sounds like the type of thing companies hang up in their offices to motivate employees. We stick them up in our office too, however one of the things my director said was this: "You can't put values on a PowerPoint slide. They're not something you present to somebody – they have to be lived. They don't need to hear it from us or see it on the wall whatever, they have to feel it by working here."

I thought it was profound. Susan David says, "Values are a quality of action. Each day we choose between actions that either moves towards our values, or away from it." She suggests something she phrased as repetitive micro-moments. If you value a relationship, for example, it is built by simple acts like giving them a hug every few mornings.

Suggesting "small, consistent actions" may finally veer this letter into self-help-book territory (it's probably already there), however my favourite part of the phrase is micro-moments. If you are wondering where in your life your values and needs are not being met, think of the moments you don't think matter, because normally that's where we can start doing things a little differently.

For instance, my quest for "purpose" sounds like a mountainous task, so I ask, What would be a micro-moment that reassures me I'm right where I'm meant to be? You'll never guess where I found it: in the stories of friends. Each time a friend shares to me something from their lives — and this can be anything from a fact to an anecdote to a whole unraveling of something they've been going through — I make space for this to be held, and I cradle it like a newborn baby. There is weight in each one, but there is also a deep well of love and gratitude for the fact that they've entrusted me with it. In these moments I think, It matters greatly that I'm here right now. And it has nothing to do with who I am, but everything to do with simply being there to hold it at the time it needed to be held.

They are micro-moments, because a few hours prior I might've been having second thoughts about grabbing that coffee or making that phone call. But it is through showing up and paying deep attention that we realise the significance of moments otherwise overlooked — moments that can make us better.

Bonus Exercise: Compassion & Delight

Finally, I might end with another idea Susan David suggested: Self-compassion.
 

"In a world where we're in a never-ending competition, self compassion may feel like you are weak or lazy or lying to yourself. That you're letting yourself 'off the hook'. But it's simply the recognition that it is hard to human. Every single one of us is being asked to human in a way that doesn't come with a playbook. A core component of our capacity to be healthy is to see yourself as a being in that struggle. How might we do this? I invite you to ask yourself: What does the 5-year-old in me need right now? Are they asking to be seen, to be loved, to feel worthy? To have spontaneity and joy?"


To tell you the truth, I grapple with these "inner child" advices because it's a running joke that childhood is the scapegoat every therapist uses. But what Susan is referring to is something called, in psychological practice, the continuity of the self. 

We all carry younger versions of ourselves within us to a certain extent, and these younger selves have hopes, dreams, and aspirations that may have dissipated as we grew older. They probably clash with our older, more responsible selves who thinks far more rationally and tends to rein back our own desires. Continuity is about strengthening that thread by keeping our sense of hope and wonder alive, without necessarily being the ballerina/astronaut our 5-year-old dreams dictated.

Say your chosen word was boredom, and as someone who values growth, your micro-moment of choice is listening to self-help podcasts while you do time-consuming chores around the house. You add a splash of delight, so every couple of instances, you replace the podcast with a Beyoncé album. You dance like the person in a vacuum commercial. You level up in silly, but also in delight.

Adopting the whimsical way a 5-year-old thinks and acts around themselves and the world is a practice in lending ourselves the compassion to loosen our own rigidity around what we feel we should do. Again, there is no playbook. And it's a long journey to rebuild. We might as well make it worthwhile, right?
