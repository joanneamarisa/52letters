23. The finiteness of it all

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 28 September 2022.

My Dear Friend,

I'm going to tell you something – but you have to promise not to make fun of me. And not to laugh. (Although it's not like I'd be able to tell.)

Every morning when I go to work, I take the Frankston train that runs southeast from the city. When the train nears Ormond station – which is where I stop – it passes by an oval football field placed in the centre of an otherwise quaint, boring old neighbourhood.

Not a lot happens on it — 9 AM on Mondays to Wednesdays is probably too early in the day and week for anything exciting. But I make a point to look at it. For some reason, every morning I remind myself to always look out to this empty football field on my way to work.

Unremarkable as it may be — save that a mum is there with a stroller or someone is playing frisbee with their Labrador — it has become sort of a morning ritual. A good luck charm, maybe, if you will.

If I don't — like this morning, as I was too entrenched in my book — I feel a bit at odds. Silly, of course, I doubt the universe conspires against me because I forgot to look at a specific spot outside a train window. But we all constantly look for something to cling onto, don't we?

Anyway, so why am I telling you this now?

I've written to you before about noticing. How in my final weeks before leaving Melbourne, I clung onto every piece that I could. There will always be something a bit endearing, a bit heartbreaking, about noticing. But we can't help it. We savour every moment, especially when we're confronted by the finiteness of it all. 

The gist is this: I have 10 months left on my work visa. Well, 10 months, 2 weeks, and 2 days to be exact. I shall be on a (one-way) plane well before then, passing duty-free aisles like any other tourist. Except this time, as I pretend to browse for travel neck pillows and candy bars at WHSmith, I'd be quietly bracing myself for another significant life leap.

To tell you the truth, I am wildly elated. At the same time, absolutely mortified.

What I do afterwards is a tomorrow question. The today question, of course, is: What do I do now? How do I live, now, finally confronted by the finiteness of it all? The beauty of everyday things followed by the brutal reminder that this is not mine for long?

A friend had asked me a similar question once. They had texted me one day, heartbroken after realising one of their relatives has been diagnosed with cancer. "Do I distance myself from them?" they asked, "Or do I spend even more time with them, knowing it would make it hurt even more?"

Any sudden loss is tragic. But any loss you have the luxury of seeing coming: Allow yourself to grieve, but also consider the time in-between a gift.

I said to my friend, "I would spend the time."

The potential impact of loss or change may daunt us, and urge us to hide, escape, or numb and protect ourselves. I'm proposing, I guess, to go towards the wave. That the impact will hit you, in fact harder, but may we never be so distracted by possible losses that we lose sight of what has always been present.

Joy, of course.

Even in the face of uncertainty, you can let life entertain you. So I suspect that's what you'll find me doing: Sitting on my train rides, looking out windows searching for good luck charms, reading stacks of books that I have no idea how to carry back home. Listening to friends share stories about their wondrously different worlds. I will be spending my time. 

And it begins, my — not week, but year of noticing. A year that I'm determined to come out of on the other side, triumphant. (For what victory, I still do not know.)

Oh, but if you were wondering, today turned out a very good day, actually. Despite missing the football field.

So... maybe we'll all be OK?

I hope you take that leap you've been waiting for. Once you are ready, of course.
Know I am rooting for you always, from this side of the world.
