June 11, 2023

41. Don't hesitate. Give in.

by Joanne Amarisa
Somewhere in the sky past the Pacific Ocean. 
Written by hand on my iPad on Sunday, 4 June 2023.
Transcribed today, 11 June 2023.
 
"If you suddenly and unexpectedly feel joy, don’t hesitate. Give in to it. There are plenty of lives and whole towns destroyed or about to be. We are not wise, and not very often kind. And much can never be redeemed. Still, life has some possibility left. Perhaps this is its way of fighting back, that sometimes something happens better than all the riches or power in the world.
It could be anything, but very likely you notice it in the instant when love begins. Anyway, that’s often the case. Whatever it is, don’t be afraid of its plenty. Joy is not made to be a crumb."

—Mary Oliver
 
My Dearest Friend,

Do you remember my first ever letter? I wrote about how, in the span of a month, I was on this jubilant burst of new activity: 

Between pre-Christmas to January, I had signed up for a dance class, pottery workshop, a 6-week barre intensive, and chopped my hair the shortest it's been. Smaller beginnings also ensued, such as buying new jeans or sipping my first espresso martini.

One and a half years later, I find myself in a similar state. Today, I'm pinching myself still over the events of the past several weeks since I last wrote to you. I tried bouldering for the first time, and then rollerblading for the first time. I turned 24, signed up to run a 10K fundraiser with my best friend, started a new relationship with someone I really, really like (more on this later), and now, jetting off on a Sunday evening to see my friends in Singapore.
 
Closing my eyes at my first attempt to sleep on this flight, I couldn't help but smile. On my right arm is a tattoo I never thought I'd get. In my ears, a Laufey love song I never thought I'd relate to.

What are the odds?

Well, plenty of odds. All these new, exciting things have something in common: Each one made me deeply question whether or not I was "cut out for it". Whether or not I was 100% ready before committing.

I haven't gone on a run in a very long time. My upper arm strength is, uh... let's say shy... so my first bouldering experience only consisted of 4 beginner-level climbs that left my hands red and sore. For 7 years I've harboured long-held beliefs when it came to the prospect of a new relationship: What if it ends badly? Or in hurt? 

I even nearly didn't join this trip to Singapore, insisting to my friends that I'm hesitant about taking time off work, before finally giving in.

So then, why am I here? On a flight, arms still sore, butterflies in my belly, and the happiest I've ever been? 

The only possible reason, of course, dear Friend, is that I have gone rogue, and slightly delirious. 

"Are you crazy???" my inner gremlin says. You don't know what you're doing! You're not cut out for this!

These changes set sirens off in my mind, paired with a joyful elevator tune. It's very offbeat in there.

Perhaps the gremlin is right. I certainly would not consider myself, right now, as someone who could run a 10K, or climb a freaking wall, or fall in love.

But perhaps that is the point.

...

An interesting story on why I joined that 10K: I have always wanted to be good at running. For years I would have a few months of consistently going on runs, and then I'd travel or the weather would get cold or I'd grow tired of it or for whatever reason, I'd eventually stop. And months later, I slowly get back to it again. And this would happen yearly.

The reason I've always wanted to be a decent runner is because, deep down, I've always wanted to run a marathon. But of course, it has always lived in a vague, distant future—or rather, alternate universe where I am perfectly fit and have built a flawless habit of running everyday at 6AM and can run miles without losing my breath.

As I was saying this to my boyfriend when we were on a hike together, he suggested, "You kind of have to sign up for one first, right? Like sign up now, and that way you've at least made the commitment to a registration fee, and then you're motivated to reach a goal."

Casually he said that, and off he went down the mountain path. While I stopped in my tracks. Huh! Why hadn't I thought of it that way?

Because in my mind, I was preoccupied with making myself qualified (read: perfect) before doing the big scary commitment. I'm still someone who needs to train up to a seamless 5K, let alone a 10. But, my plan has been flipped on its head. Instead of willing and waiting myself to embody a runner and then commit "when I'm ready", I did the big thing first, and now have to follow through.

We will rarely feel ready or qualified for the things we want most. But there is an alternative—a bolder route: To say yes first. To do the big scary commitment first, and through progress and practice, work towards being qualified, rather than waiting around assessing our own ability and worth before taking any action. 

To not feel cut out for it, and do it anyway. To let the delirium motivate us to something beyond what we'd imagine ourselves to become.

Perhaps the trick is to not wait. To let my little insecurity gremlin sink its teeth into my calf, and take it running with me. To let it latch onto my back, as I carry it up with me climbing that goddamn wall. I have long disqualified myself from life experiences before actually experiencing them, out of fear of failure, rejection, heartbreak... As I enter 24, I don't want to hesitate.

I want to give in.
...
 
I unwrapped a birthday present earlier this morning—a checkered scarf from a dear friend. In her card, she reminded me of the Mary Oliver quote above, Joy is not made to be a crumb.

Piggybacking off of that, the birthday-week reflections I have to share with you, aren't too far off from what I said in my last letter: Our time ends sooner or later. We're not on this earth forever, and it shall still orbit and bloom and turn and babies will still laugh and autumns will still be beautiful and waterfalls will still roar and cities will still be built, before and after us.

And this fact is not to scare us, or to point us to nihilistic apathy. But to recognise that my every minute, my working limbs, this beating heart—though it carries with it flaws and grief—are all a package of undeserved gifts, not meant for me to keep for myself.

And when you recognise that fact, that all your life is but a product of grace, is when change starts to happen. It's like an anchor gets hooked to the base of my heart, and my whole being now harbours a new gravity.

A gravity where I couldn't afford to wait. I couldn't afford to think twice, three times, four times over whether or not I'm worth it, or cut out for it, or can do or say the right and perfect thing. Regardless of the answer, I need to get moving.

So in the 72 hours of my birthday weekend (and yep, I extended past the usual 24-hours this year because I can), I sat and ate and laughed and hugged the wonderful rays of sun I get to call my friends. I run and swim and climb and feel the silliest and most vulnerable I've ever felt, but also the most alive. I grip onto the hand of someone who told me they chose me, and I make the conscious choice to believe them. 

In the words of Cheryl Strayed, "Don't be strategic or coy. Be brave. Be authentic. We're all going to die, Johnny. Hit the iron bell like it's dinnertime."

Don't hesitate. Give in. The odds are always going to be against us, friends. Better tango with it while we can.
