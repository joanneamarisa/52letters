37. How to end the day

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Tuesday, 11 April 2023.

My Dear Friend,

Today, we lose. There are days where you come home victorious, where you leap into bed thinking, "Ah, today was great!" But today is not one of those days.

How did the soldiers come home when the war was over, but lost? How must we come home when the world sends us away, defeated? Because here we are on a weeknight, marching, like ants, through the tunnels back to our little nests, wondering why or how or what if. We wanted so much to conquer, but alas, the day is slipping away from us already.

So go then. Make your way home. But before that, as you finally stand up from your desk, sling your bag over your shoulder ready to leave, please say goodbye.

Say bye to someone: a janitor, a friend, the downstairs concierge. Make sure you say good night to the colleague who still has their laptop open – you might be their reminder that it's time to go home, too. Make sure you smile to whoever's inside the elevator when it dings at your floor. And make sure you breathe, on those first few steps outside of the building. Take deep breaths if you must. Your best has been enough. And if it wasn't enough today, you'll have a second chance tomorrow.

On your commute — and you might be stuck in the congested streets of Jakarta or standing shoulder-to-shoulder with strangers on a train in Melbourne — look up. No, seriously. This is the part of the day that everyone would prefer to fast forward. But pay attention. Stay engaged to something. A book, a podcast, a playlist, the view from the window. We mark the hours spent in transit as wasted or lost time, but it doesn't have to be.

Now hopefully your book or your podcast or your playlist or the things you've seen from the window have been enough to unlatch your mind from work. (Or did I just make you think about work by mentioning it again?) But in case it hasn't, and the worries follow you home, leave them, politely, by the foot of your doorstep.

I have no authority to bar you, of course, if you decide to come home, sit on your couch, open your laptop and work some more. But I've personally found it healthier to do the alternative: To wring it dry and place it by the door, like the soaked umbrella that it is, which you know would just drip all over your floors if you decided to bring it inside.

Fatigue is a bad place to try and be productive. It's also a bad place to try to be creative, or engaged, or patient, or loving. So may I suggest, letting your fatigue be what it is: a signal, that your body and mind needs some replenishing. You are allowed to cater to that need. You're not at the office now. You don't need to answer to anybody but yourself.

So take a shower. Even on dreary weekends where I'm staying at home and finding it hard to start a house chore or a task, my motto is always, Start with a shower. Whatever the day brought, whatever you have or haven't done, you deserve to be clean. Douse yourself in water, let it be warm (or cold – you do you), and come out with fresher eyes, feeling like you're in a new skin.

Make yourself dinner. I don't care whether it's a lasagna or leftovers or instant ramen type of night. But my tip? Always have some go-to garnishes in the kitchen. Think sesame seeds, bits of herbs or chives, "nothing" vegetables like lettuce or cabbage, dried seaweed, or even an egg. You'll be surprised how a small touch or a sprinkle can make a meal look, and taste, better, whatever that meal is. Now, it's not just an I need to eat so let's get it over with - but I've made myself a dinner.

Switch off in these hours, if you can. Namely because the world is loud as it is — you heard it out there — and most of the time, you don't need those voices repeated to you by your phone screen. The news will still be there the next morning, and your assumptions about them are probably right: someone is on a trip, someone did get engaged, and a celebrity couple has broken up. That's it! You're caught up for the day!

Reduce to a minimum anything that doesn't help you calm down. With your dinner, you can turn on your favourite TV show, spend half the meal choosing something on Netflix, or let someone know out of the blue that you're thinking of them. (I mean... you must've been thinking about something?)

You can also read a book, or have a dinner-call with someone you love, and tell them about – oh, that's right – your terrible terrible day.

And finally, when the dishes are in the sink (or are washed and on the drying rack, you responsible adult you), it's time for bed. Take that book or that phone call or that Netflix show with you, if you'd like. And before you sleep, just remember: Everything you've done has been enough. Everything you haven't done is forgiven.

Find one thing you're grateful for that day, and it can be something profound like having a job, something simple like your breath, or something stupid like your fingernails. And if that was too easy, aim for three, or five. You can do even numbers too, of course, who am I to decide.

And this evening, or on evenings that leave you more crushed than others — we each get our fair share of them — I hope you'll find crumpled in your pocket, or sitting in your inbox, this letter. Not a promise to make it all better, but a salve. A reminder that on days when you feel utterly energy- and time-poor, by slowing down, paying attention, and not letting go of life's littlest pleasures, we can still make ourselves a little richer.
