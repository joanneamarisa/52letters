21. Why I'm learning to be less nice

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 15 September 2022.

My Dear Friend,

When you're in your 20s, a question you come across time and time again is: Where do you see yourself in the next 5-10 years? 

I come across this question a few times a year. But as I navigate work life and pay bills and do this sing-and-dance of 'gradulthood', I realise the question I actually feel confronted with every day is: Who do I want to be in the next 5-10 years?

It's not as cliche as it sounds. I think of this question more practically: However way I make people feel nowadays, 5 years from now, I could be 5x better at making them feel that way. In whatever stage of life I'm in now, I'm developing patterns in work, relationships, and thought processes that will inevitably grow and cultivate in 5-10 years time.

So do I like where I'm going? Or am I still cynical when I do some work and don't get the credit?

Rather than asking whether I'm on the proverbial "right track", I'm more curious on: Do my co-workers enjoy working with me? Do my friends feel loved and know I care about them? Am I appreciative of people's time? How is my work ethic recently? Am I someone who does what I say I'll do?

Hard, but important assessments. Where do you see yourself suggests a destination. It suggests our lives are scaled by steps of achievements and we want to arrive at a certain step in that ladder by an X amount of time. It's great for goal-setting, but real life doesn't always work like this — and if I keep thinking that it does, I'll feel entitled to a world where things go exclusively my way. Whereas who do I want to be offers a softer, more malleable metric: In any outcome my life unfolds to, I'd like to come out as someone bearable to be around, you know?

So 5 years from now, who would I like to be? To answer this question, I try and learn from the people I look up to.

I'm not talking about celebrities, influencers, or figures that you see two-dimensionally. I think it's important to have a list of people you admire, highly respect, and can learn from — and ideally, they are people you know. People whose struggles, wins, and characters you can see the ins and outs of, rather than people whose lives you scroll through.

I have a handful that come to mind (mostly mentors, educators, leaders I've had the honour of working with.) I try to pull a red thread on what they all share in common. I found three traits worth noting. Some food for thought?
 
...

1. They are constant learners.

I'm talking... constant. You know that feeling when you're speaking with someone and you think, Wow, I can learn so much from this person? Well, there are people who just radiate that, and I've always been intrigued by how.

I realise this is because they themselves are constant learners. My manager, for instance, often chirps about, "Oh, I read an article the other day-" or "Oh, I taught myself how to do that! Took me weeks, but I finally got to it."

This inspires me because it means that even though some of them are academics and/or have years of experience under their belt, no one on this list seems to see themselves as 'having arrived'. They constantly expand and deepen said knowledge in the humility that there's always more to uncover, and this is why passing on or exuding that knowledge is something that comes so naturally to them.

Food for thought: Am I still learning in my spare time? Does having a degree and a job make me complacent in my learning?

2. They don't worry about being "nice"

They are some of the most generous and kindest people I know. But — and maybe because they are mentors, managers, lecturers — they aren't frivolous with their time. In fact, they seem to safeguard their time and energy quite well. In doing so, they make sure they are at their most useful when people need them. They are straightforward, direct, but sincere.

This is something I'm trying to adopt: Being less people-pleasing nice.

Less "It's ok if not!" nice.

Less "I'm sorry to bother you" or "Please let me know if this makes sense" or "Sorry please don't worry if it would take too long to check" nice.

I'm ashamed to say this past month is when I have finally stopped writing emails like this. I can be so preoccupied sometimes with "how I come across" (in both personal & professional settings) which is classic people-pleaser behaviour.

I learned it's kinder to be direct. It is much more helpful to be blunt in the ways you can and cannot help, or ways you do or do not need help. And at the end, a more effective use of your time means being able to help more people in one day.

Food for thought: Are you still apologetic when communicating your needs? What good do you feel you gain by "softening" your presence and voice? What is the worst thing that could happen if you stopped doing it?

3. They're honest about what they can offer the world

They are direct, but these leaders are also honest when they don't know the answer! In doing so, they model to me what it means to live authentically and not subject yourself to being everything for everyone. (Because no one can be everything to everyone.)

I used to think that I would look up to the Bill Gates-es of the world — high-figure innovators, philanthropists, pioneers... and maybe we all have a collective awe for people who fit in these categories. But observing the people on my list, it's not prominence that invites me to admire & respect them.

One of them wants to master digital marketing, two are academics in Fine Art, one is deeply invested in her work as a book publisher and educator. Their gift to the world is their love for the work. They take full ownership of the work, take great pride in the work, and are also honest about limitations of the work. As a result, I sense they are excited about their respective vocations, and seem very at peace with their chosen paths. 

For example: If I work in business, I am actually allowed to put 110% effort into growing businesses and making money. It's actually fine if saving the planet is not my ultimate career goal — as long as I don't claim that it is. We cannot all save whales and overturn bureaucratic governments and create the new iPhone — something that plagues the woke early-20-year-olds who want to do a hundred different things to change the world. Instead, we ought to reconsider our limiting beliefs about what it means to "bring good" into the world.

We are useful to the world when we are true to ourselves, and invested in work that matters to us.

Food for thought: Do you speak to yourself & others honestly about the true intentions of your work? Do you often feel inadequate because you're comparing yourself to arbitrary measures of success that may or may not be important to you?
