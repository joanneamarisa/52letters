24. Unexpected joys

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 21 October 2022.

My Dearest Friend,

Oh, what a month. How is your October? I hope it's been fun.

Last week, I spent much of my time in my room; an ominous thunderstorm has been passing over Melbourne recently. But after several days of being cooped up at home, I suddenly woke up last Friday to a bright, sunny morning. Pleasantly surprised, in 15 minutes I was already in my sneakers and out the door.

I hadn't visited Fawkner Park in a long time – it's my favourite, but I'm sure you've seen me gush about it before. However, when I finally reached it, I realised that in one of their fields — on which I sometimes see people playing cricket or football — there was a construction site.

A fact about me you don't need: I hate construction sites. I hate seeing them wherever they randomly appear. The cold, uninviting steel barriers that would one day obstruct my sidewalk, paired with those orange cones or little flag buntings. Pedestrians go this way! Go where, exactly?

When I see them obstruct a place I consider part of my routine, a part of me whines like a child, No no no! How dare you! They even had a crane and practically bulldozed the green, grassy field into a massive dirt patch. The dirt stretches the width and length of the field, now barred by a construction fence.

Passing the fence, I learn they are installing new seating areas and improving the sports field for better access. For the rest of that sunny walk, this is all I thought about: It is similar to what I feel God does to our hearts sometimes.

As a churchgoer, there are moments in life where I feel I've spent many enough years and Sundays and think, Oh, I've seen it all. That God has done all He's needed to do with me.

Of course, I learn it isn't true. From past to future, on both our best and worst moments, we are in need of saving. The work of salvation doesn't cease. And that includes being saved from ourselves, and the things we harbour that we don't address.

Encountering Christ is indeed to be plowed through. It can be devastating, because something about receiving a love you don't feel like you deserve is heartbreaking, if not difficult to believe. And at times, choosing to let God heal us will feel as painful as this mud-covered football field. Who likes all this dirt exposed? Nothing to see here, God! Look away!

But much like the football field, when we bring down our facades and finally recognise our need for grace — when we let our hearts truly break, knowing it is in His good hands — healing and restoration begin.

I don't like the inconvenience of healing the same way I don't like the inconvenience of construction sites. I have to make room for it, and trust that the process is to restore what needed to be restored. It is inconvenient and uncomfortable, but necessary. The ugly is the necessary. 

And you won't believe what I found next. After turning a corner to the other side of the field, I realise some parts of the mud would dip slightly deeper into the ground than others. And because of the heavy rain, this formed large puddles in parts of the dug up field compared to its other areas.

The water in those puddles reflect the skies and trees above it — which now look bright, and leafy, and the sky is just so blue. And you see all of it in this weird pasture of dirt that has now partly become a temporary man-made pond, with sunlit rainwater puddled in the midst of it.

There is beauty in the pain. When we pay closer attention to seasons we impatiently want to get out of, we may find ourselves in awe. Like this massive puddle, in a dirt patch, that reflects the clouds and the trees of my most favourite park — walking with God brings with it unexpected joys.

We are not promised ease, but we are promised a Love that doesn't abandon us. That fights for us, and just wants to give us a chance. And isn't that what we all look for?

It's not because of our sudden worthiness. The way of the world programs us to think we have to live up to the kind of love that accepts us as we are. That we can be loved, if we can somehow earn it. 

But the scandal of grace is that we don't even have to live up to any of it. (Thank goodness, because I never can.) The call is simply to trust, and to receive. Long before any 'work' takes place, He already considers us worthy.

My favourite Bible verse is 2 Corinthians 12:9. But he said to me, “My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." 

For grace to flood in, our weakness offers the perfect ground. Isn't that good news? Aren't we all so lucky?

Unexpected joys.
