31. Kindness

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Friday, 17 February 2023.

My Dear Friend,

Between wrapping up my final weeks at my workplace, scheduling movers for an apartment, taking our cat to the vet, and navigating the Facebook Marketplace dungeon of used furniture and erratic buyers, you can say I'm a little stressed, haha.

Today I buckled. After waiting all morning for movers that never came (a long story that I don't feel like reliving), I finally went home and was ready to just... give up on the day.

Living in a foreign country is like this: on good days, it's beautiful. On hard days, it's really hard.

So there I was, at the doorstep of my apartment, rummaging for keys when, from the inside, the man who is one of the cleaners of our building peered at me through the glass doors. He is a short-haired man, probably in his 30s, who doesn't say much except when we exchange polite nods of "hello"s each time we pass each other on my floor.

Every Friday I see him vacuuming, head down, earbuds in. That moment, he recognised me and opened the access for me from the inside. And in I went with one of my boxes of clothes (and probably sweat on the side of my face).

"Thank you so much," I said, and he returned a smile.

Next, I went to the lobby to see the building manager, having to tell her I'm rescheduling the move. Until now, I don't know the woman's name. She signs her emails as Building Manager and that's it. One day though I was taking empty cardboard boxes downstairs to throw away, when she passed me and said, "Actually — can I take that! Can I take this box? I'll tell you why; my daughter, she has these Christmas statues, that she's trying to put away. And they're the perfect height!" She was so glad about it too. So that's everything I know about her.

I stepped at her office's door, which is usually left open. "Excuse me, hi!" She swiveled her chair back to me. 

I told her what happened. "Aw, I'm sorry love," she uttered, "did you want to book another slot?" We have to book time slots for the loading bay, if we want to move furniture in. Oh, I already did! I said, On the website. "Oh yeah, actually I think I saw that come through," she mentioned.

I told her the person with the next bookingcan start earlier and take over, if they'd like.

"Oh that's very kind," she replied, smiling sympathetically. "I'll let them know, that's very kind."

"What apartment number are you in?" she continued, "Did you have a parcel waiting?" There was a row of parcels on her shelves.

"No, I think I'm good!" I said. She smiled, we thanked each other, I left.

I walked to the elevator, the doors closed, and I shed a tear.

On good days, it's beautiful. On hard days, it's really hard.

And on those hard days, a stranger opening the door or a lady who lends a listening ear, tremendously helps. It's only kindness that helps us navigate the overwhelm.

...

Kindness is the most distinct thing I remember from my first arrival to Melbourne.

I was 18, and I took that first "big move" flight alone. Even though I know my way around airports, I don't remember it being as aesthetic as those montages you see on Instagram Reels. Majority of the time, I walked with mixed feelings, semi-afraid, hiding my puffy eyes after saying goodbyes to the family. 

In my phone case, I kept a folded up post-it note with a handwritten step-by-step on what to do after landing, and what numbers to call. You got this, you got this, I kept thinking to myself.

But what really lit the path, was the kindness of strangers. One stranger, actually. We'll call her Diane.

We got Diane's number from a family friend, and it was written onto my post-it note. She lives near the airport, and was meant to drive me to the city. When her minivan pulled up, I was greeted by a sprightly lady with curly hair, a rainbow-coloured top, jean shorts, and sunglasses. She opened the car door.

"Hello! Good morning!" We introduced ourselves to each other and drove away. Because it was too early to collect the keys, Diane invited me to her house instead. And that's how I found myself in her kitchen at 8 in the morning, in a suburban family home that was just as comforting and messy as any.

It was summer, and sunlight was coming in from every direction. There was a long wooden shelf on the left wall of the kitchen, and Diane toured me through her travel trinkets, her family photos, greeting cards from her church. She cooked me breakfast, asked "Do you like to cook?", and proceeded to pull open a drawer full of every spice imaginable.

As with any home, there were half-eaten, rubber-banded snacks on the counter. A multi-coloured pile of jackets on the coat stand. Board games and remote controls strewn across the sofas. 

And the mess welcomed me. The smell of eggs and bacon filled the air, interlaced with Diane's many stories. "You'll like Melbourne!" she said. Her beagle was following me the whole time, and I remember how the kitchen island glowed that morning. After the cold, isolating experience I had in the 12 hours leading up to it, it was the warmest I've ever felt.
Diane's beagle in her kitchen

There were other small instances of kindness on that day too. Like a taller man who, when we were about to get off the plane, helped me grab my overhead luggage and set it down next to me, without a word.

Or a blonde-haired girl — a fellow student, I learned — who saw me arrive, and opened the door with her access key to let me in. "Are you allowed to do that?" I asked, as she held the door open for my 40kg+ of suitcases. "Mmmm, probably not. But you came all this way!" she said with a smile.

I may not have the wisdom of a locksmith or a 70-year-old Uber driver, but I think, where possible, be kind. You never know what a person's going through is correct, but also, you never know what helps. Lend a smile, hold a door open, when the barista gets your coffee order wrong, my God, if you're not allergic to it, just take it and go. What helps is often as simple as not making another person's day a little harder. 

The kindness of others allows me to be kinder to myself too. I'm reminded to live life at a more forgiving pace.

And finally, I'd like to dedicate this letter to a friend who is one of the people in this world who aces kindness. Last month, I was meeting up with her for dinner, and we agreed to meet at a park near the train station.

I am right in saying it's been a stressful year. So as I was waiting for her, I was sitting on a bench, closing my eyes and holding my jacket close to my chest. I was lost in thought about recent events in my personal life, and for 5 minutes, I just let myself feel all of it. In my mind, I walked slowly through the overwhelm. Not to pick it apart or logic my way into fixing it. Just to let myself be.

And then her message came. From a distance, I saw her smile, and rush to me from across the street. I waved back from where I was sitting, my train of thought disrupted instantly. As she reached the park, she was still smiling, and before we hugged, she was jumping up and down to see me. It has only been 4 weeks.

Between you and me, dear reader, I can't tell you how healing that was. But it is.

I didn't tell her. But I hope she knows. I hope they all do.
 

How has unexpected kindness helped you in your journey?
