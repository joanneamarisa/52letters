42. My grief & joy are roommates

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Sunday, 16 July 2023.
 
My Dear Friend,

My grief and joy are roommates. When the light floods into my living room and touches the edge of my sofa each morning, my joy is what pulls me to sit there, soaking the warmth in.

Joy opens my window, pours me coffee in the morning, humming to a Chet Baker song our grandfather loves. My grief crawls along the floors, stays in the corner of our dark bedroom, considering blankets her sanctuary from anything she doesn't feel like facing.

I call them roommates because in the home of my heart, they are forced to co-exist. And I used to think I would need to carve out so much space and time for sadness, flush it out of my system, before I can even let the slightest ray of joy to enter.

But I've learned that both are not mutually exclusive. Both grief and joy are more generous with their space than we ought to believe. Even if my grief is the type to stay in fetal position for 48 hours. Even if there was a day, earlier this year, where I laid in my friend's bedroom and cried, thinking my world had officially crumbled. 

Yet in the past month, I have found myself in many bouts of laughter. I have experienced falling in love. I've stepped out into nature thanking God with all the words I have left, for everything I witness and hear and feel and touch.

I had no idea I would find my way back to wonder, but I know now that it is possible. And I suppose I slip this letter under your doorstep to tell you, too, that it is possible.

I am learning what it means to cradle grief as I look towards a future. I am learning what it means to reckon with the heartbreak I'm harbouring, all whilst celebrating the life and people I've been graciously given.

When given the chance, grief can deepen my joy, rather than hinder it. It grounds me and reminds me to never take anything for granted.

And when given the chance, joy gives hope to my grief. In small, minuscule ways, since my grief is almost never ready. But my joy is generous with her light, and is willing to wait as I mourn. And when the two learn to communicate, we find space in the light for my sadness. We find space in the dark for my gratitude.
 
 
"Sorrow and strife are part of even a joyful life. Accept that it's going to take a long time for you to get that monster out of your chest. Accept that someday what pains you now will surely pain you less.

You're looking for the explanation, the loophole, the bright twist in the dark tale that reverses its course. Anyone would be. It's the reason I've had to narrate my own stories of injustice about seven thousand times, as if by raging about it once more the story will change. But it won't change. Allow your acceptance of that to be a transformative experience. You do that by simpling looking it square in the face and then moving on. You don't have to move fast or far. You can go just an inch. You can mark your progress breath by breath."

–Cheryl Strayed, Tiny Beautiful Things
