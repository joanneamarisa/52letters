16. Resilience, and science

by Joanne Amarisa
Surabaya, Indonesia. Saturday, 6 August 2022.
"B" (If I Should Have a Daughter)
by Sarah Kay

"If I should have a daughter... 
Instead of mom, she’s going to call me Point B,
because that way she knows that no matter what happens, she can always find her way to me.

I am going to paint the Solar Systems on the backs of her hands, so she has to learn the entire universe before she can say ‘Oh, I know that like the back of my hand.’

And she’s going to learn that this life will hit you, hard, in the face, wait for you to get back up just so it can kick you in the stomach,
but getting the wind knocked out of you is the only way to remind your lungs how much they like the taste of air.

There'll be days like this, when you open your hands to catch, and wind up with only blisters and bruises. When your boots will fill with rain and you’ll be up to your knees in disappointment. 

And those are the very days you have all the more reason to say thank you.

You will put the wind in win some … lose some,
you will put the star in starting over and over.
And no matter how many landmines erupt in a minute, be sure your mind lands on the beauty of this funny place called life.

And yes, on a scale from one to over-trusting, I am pretty damn naive.

But I want her to know that this world is made of sugar. It can crumble so easily, but don’t be afraid to stick your tongue out and taste it.
Remember that good things come in threes, and so do bad things,
and always apologise when you’ve done something wrong, but don’t you ever apologise for the way your eyes refuse to stop shining.

And when they finally hand you a heartache,
when they slip war and hatred under your door
and offer you handouts on street corners, of cynicism and defeat,
you tell them that they
really ought to meet your mother."

...
 
My Dear Friend,

I've always loved that poem. This morning, I sat at my desk and played it back again. I listen to it at least once every year.

Recently, my mother took me on her jogging route. We were passing lakes, quiet streets, and quieter neighbourhoods before the sun fully shone. 

When the morning light came, it was the colour of citrus. If houses were people, I imagined that each one was stretching its arms, rising lazily from its slumber. Opening its front doors — a yawn — and drawing the curtains of its windows — a pair of eyes, batting awake.

As the morning grew busier, I thought about Sarah's poem; about resilience, how life is both beautiful and brutal, and about how resilience finds us. Resilience happens when, as is often the case, the world doesn't follow the scripts we write for it. It's about not getting what you wanted. About heartache. About things unraveling the way you didn't expect it to.

In the words of Sarah Kay, this hits you hard, "in the face, waits for you to get back up just so it can kick you in the stomach."

It's funny because two thoughts occurred to me:

1) That I've never considered myself as someone who is resilient. Quite the contrary, I'm annoyingly sensitive to everything that happens to me and around me, and I equate this softness with a lack of toughness.

But 2) I also discover that I am more surprised when things go the way I planned, compared to when they don't. 

As you can see, my two theories don't match. Am I acclimatised to change, or fragile to change? Am I too emotionally attached to what I want, or am I so detached that when they happen, I'm taken by surprise? 

When there is something I've wanted for a long time, I name the emotion longing. It's like wanting, but wanting almost painfully. High school kids waiting for their crush to text back. Candidates waiting to hear back from a job interview.  Children seeing unopened gifts with their name on it on Christmas Eve. Actors before their big break. Two people getting ready for their first time meeting.

Similar to loving, longing also opens us to the possibility of failure or rejection. The higher we hope, the further the stretch is between us and disappointment — like a diving board that grows taller and taller with every wishful thinking. The deeper the fall.

You either plunge into cool, refreshing water where what you long for comes true, or you land with a painful blow, where you question your own survival. Understandably, this incites a fear of the fall. When I find myself longing for something, fear of it not coming true soon follows. 

My initial reaction is to talk myself out of it. My brain trials many computations of scenarios, and I know exactly what it's doing: It's trying to shield me from disappointment. It's trying to outsmart the fall. When I am yearning for a certain outcome, it turns from wishing for an outcome, to being daunted by the outcome, to wilfully rejecting the outcome so I don't become devastated by the outcome. 

As Sarah Kay suggests, we live in a world that tells us to never get our hopes up. "If you don't expect much, you will never be disappointed," were the words of Spiderman's MJ, I think.

But I've always found that too nihilistic for my taste. If we remain a person who lets fear stop us from being hopeful, is that not as equally devastating as falling?

Which brings me back to resilience. Facing the fears attached to our longing is human. My hands shake. My chest feels like it's already in mid-air. I feel a deep pit in my stomach, like it's been scooped out and left hollow. Fear is like this, so longing is like this too.

But when our fears – be it failure or rejection or loss or a combination of either – happen, how do we survive it? When we had our palms open yet the world still handed us heartache, when we open our doors yet it leaves us flooded with defeat. When we've done the hard work of hoping, yet the impact still breaks us, how do we move past it?

And most importantly, how do we mend the wreckage?

...

One night at 11:30pm, my friend texted me, "I have a question." Minutes before, we were laughing about something else. Within seconds of that text, she and I were discussing relationships, and loving, and the fears it incites in the human heart.

I realise what plagues many people who have spent lifetimes being 'independent' is we fear what happens when we open ourselves up to hope and love, because we all know what's on the other side of them too. 

It's hard to let go of self-reliance. "I'm scared that I'll get hurt," she stated. "I'm scared I'll be disappointed."

I paused for a long time, pondering on what she said. Is the possibility of getting hurt enough reason to simply not try, not hope, at all? Out of nowhere, I replied.

It's actually less about trusting the other person, I said. It's about trusting our own strength. 

When we're scared of letting people down, of hurting others, of causing sadness and all these other fears, I think it's a lack of trust in ourselves.

I don't trust that people 'will never let me down', I continued, they inevitably will, including people we love and commit our whole lives to. 

But whatever happens, I know I won't let myself down. Whatever happens, I know I'll be there for me.

I've always been there for me.

An advice that liberated the giver, perhaps more than the receiver. That the leap is often worth the fall. That even in the fall, we don't need to question our survival, because the fact that we are here is evidence of our ability to survive at all. 

Is it not strength to be forging onwards, texting our best friends about our darkest fears at 11:30pm?

Earlier today, I recalled a science term: Homeostasis. It's how the body is always working to reach its stable condition. You start shivering when your body gets cold, for instance — what you lack, such as heat, your body will work to regain. Homeostasis is the state of balance our body's systems work towards, in order to survive.

In order to survive.

I can't help but think of those shaking hands. The symptoms I considered were my body's reaction to the ache of longing. How weak I always feel in those moments, before the leap, not knowing what happens after hoping. Similarly, when we get chills, or break out in fever, we think it's the body breaking, accepting defeat — when in fact, it's the body holding on.

A small science lesson turned into a reminder on resilience. My two previous theories now land on somewhat common ground:

1) That sensitivity is not a weakness. The body relies on sensitivity to tell it what it needs, so it can perform.

2) Every fibre in us is built to adapt, including (and especially) through unpredicted, unexpected scenarios.

We are living, breathing testimonies to our own resilience. It's easy for us to look back on the times we've crumbled, or made regrettable mistakes, or standing with buckled knees because we don't want to jump into the swimming pool. Yet we recall these seemingly fragile moments not realising we are forged iron. Tempered, hardened, without necessarily losing our softer, human edges.

Symptoms of our fear may actually be symptoms of gaining strength. I don't exactly know how we mend the wreckage. I don't think we need to be told how.

I suspect we have done it so, so many times.
