13. What makes you laugh?

by Joanne Amarisa
Surabaya, Indonesia. Monday, 4 July 2022.

"My mother is devotion itself. She was the one with my father at every doctor’s appointment, the one who sat with him in every hospital, who made him laugh, who held his hand.
My parents came back together just in time to lose each other. As we were cleaning out my father’s basement, my mother told me that I should marry someone I can laugh with."

— Tatiana Jackson-Saitz
A Texting Lifeline During a Difficult Time (2022), Modern Love for the New York Times.


​My Dearest Friend,

I found it interesting, that in the anonymous readership survey I sent to you for Letters from Jo, a topic that 80% of you wanted to hear more of, was love. Relationships — from the platonic, familial, to romance — the breadth of which wouldn't fit in a single letter.

I don't know if being single makes me least qualified, or most qualified to write about it, but I thought I'd start with at least one piece of advice I know to be true: Find someone who makes you laugh.

It applies to first dates, new friendship circles, meeting co-workers in a new workplace, scanning for a cousin you can get along with at the family gathering... Every time someone asks me about dating dealbreakers, I always mention, "Sense of humour is top priority."

Ironically, I take the ability to not take oneself too seriously, very seriously. It's not about finding someone who's a jokester, really, but I think sharing a laugh signals effortless compatibility. When we laugh, we are in a state of openness. We only laugh when we feel comfortable, and light. In a world of facades, it's the unspoken permission to finally let your guard down. And you deserve a life you can live with your guard down.

When interacting with someone new, try to observe what they laugh about. (But also try to do this without crossing into psycho-analysing... I know, I know, just... try.)

What people find funny is actually very telling, both about who they are and about your potential alikeness. For example, a few years ago I went out with someone who would craft jokes all the time. At one point, he laughed about a person's disability, and this was one of the first laughs we didn't share. (I didn't like him much after that. We stopped dating, but as the universe would have it, he is now a stand-up comedian.)

A different example: My co-workers and I laugh at ourselves many times, about mundane things like phrasing a sentence awkwardly, misreading an email, or being nitpicky when it comes to feedback. At first when I was starting out, facing these mistakes made me nervous. Now, I've joined them in the plains of: Oh Jo, you're just one person! I can attest this work culture bears fruit — we form a trusting camaraderie, and are less stressed with ourselves during high-pressure situations.

I personally love starting big presentations with a: "Phew! I'm nervous!" Just to see everyone smile or laugh. Again, a practice in being seen. After doing so, I can proceed while breathing a little more easily. Sometimes I can tell that breaking the ice catches people off guard, but it makes life less charade-like. Another person I can think of whose humour catches people off guard is my father. 

Dad had this "gotcha!" sense of humour. He was a big man and most of my friends who didn't know him thought he was intimidating. This was odd to me, knowing the man as a warm, generous jester with plenty of tricks up his sleeve. Out of everyone in the presence of his joke, though, he was always the one most entertained, laughing the longest at something he came up with himself. Maybe that's a Dad thing.

My mother's humour, on the other hand, teeters on the edge of wise and self-deprecating, with a mild hint of "Oh, isn't life always like that!" Somewhat reminiscent of women in 1950s Hollywood films. Like any mother, she is tough as nails; facing life's curveballs with a deep breath and a good laugh afterwards. I don't know how she does it, but I'll tell you once I find out.

I'm trying to follow her cue. Training myself to laugh about things not going the way I planned, is my new weapon for handling unexpected grievances; plans being cancelled, applications getting rejected. It loosens my attachment, and in turn, I feel less offended when life hands me an annoying jack-in-the-box surprise. 

When I was 17 and enduring my first break-up, I was devastated. I went to school each morning wrapped in a hoodie with my head down and earphones on. Very unlike me, as friends once testified. As a teenager, there were so many moments that felt like the sure end of the world. And yet, by the healing work of female friendships, a few months later, Jo was laughing again. 

I don't know what or who or how. Only that in every laughter, I felt invincible.

In reply to this letter, tell me — what makes you laugh?
