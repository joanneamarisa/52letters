32. Beautiful losses

by Joanne Amarisa

Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 1 March 2023.
 
My Dearest Friend,

It was my last day of work yesterday. As excited as I am to start at a new workplace tomorrow, I am also mourning the loss of my old job today. And that's okay!

Yesterday my office team surprised me with a bouquet of fresh flowers with a bag of goodies as a proper goodbye. One I absolutely did not expect and sent me home feeling so grateful. I'm lucky to have left on such terms, and to come away from my (first) office job with good lessons and fond memories.

It feels a little like grief! I don't know if "mourning" or "grieving" are the right words — they are tied to very devastating pictures of loss, like black veils and cries at a funeral. Whereas mine is captured in this batch of soft pink, green, and white flowers I have wrapped around my arms, remembering the smiles of my colleagues as I wait for the (last ever) train to arrive.

As I look ahead into the year (psshh.. whatever that means), I've lived long enough to know that losing is also part of the bargain. In letting go, we step into newness. So there has to be room for it amidst our ambitions to progress, to start new chapters or become new selves. Because you can't have growth without the losses. Instead, loss generously makes room for our hope.

Someone walking into a marriage may think about losing their singleness. Someone moving to a new country may ruminate about what they're leaving behind. In my case — aside from my job — my housemate had dropped off the keys to our old apartment earlier this week.

An apartment we spent about 2–3 years in. On its final day, I stepped in after the cleaners scrubbed the place up. There are no stains on the kitchen counter, and I think of those kimchi stir fries or beef soups we concocted at this very stove. All the gossip, the life updates we shared just by standing at 3 opposite sides of the kitchen — one of us near the fridge, one at the doorway, and one at the snack cabinet — and we could easily spend 30 minutes on a weeknight just chatting right there.

The carpets were steamed and spotless. The bedrooms were left empty, doors ajar, and I remember how at each doorway we would wish the other person a good luck — for an upcoming test, a job application, a visa extension. And we'd complain about the noise, and the light bulbs that didn't work, and the cheap door locks, and the heat when it's summer time. But memories had seeped into the walls of that three-bedroom flat, and I tried my best to bring as much as I can with me, as I finally left and closed its door for the last time.

Sometimes losses aren't as romantic — only sickening and devastating the moment they happen. But with the several that I've had throughout my lifetime, given enough time, I'm able to realise that they, too, were grace. Time gives meaning to loss — not necessarily reason, but just an assurance that it had to happen, to take us to where we are now.

My first two months of the year are dotted with beautiful losses. Yet these jarring changes to my personal and professional life must only mean that better things are coming. In fact, I see them trickling in. It just takes a bit of hope, a bit of faith, to see them.

I pray that you also see yours.

What is your meaning to loss?
