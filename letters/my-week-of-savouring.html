May 18, 2022

My week of savouring

by Joanne Amarisa

Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 18 May 2022.
How do you measure, measure a year?
In daylights, in sunsets, in midnights, in cups of coffee
In inches, in miles, in laughter, in strife

My Dear Friend,

My final weeks in Melbourne before flying back home is full of savouring. In the knowledge that by this weekend I would be miles and miles away, all my morning commutes and mundane tasks are awash with sentimentality. (It doesn't help that every street is giving me autumn vibes. What a parting gift!)
 
Sunday: Two Sundays prior, I was catching up with a friend of mine who was also about to depart overseas. We meet over brunch and coffee in a place called Journeyman. We chat about our jobs, our trips, money, savings. At the end, she hugs me goodbye, and it strikes us both that we wouldn't see each other until August. So she hugs me again, a second time.

Thursday: Eight-ish days to go, I make a store visit to organise a small event for the company I work for. The store manager greets me, and she has the kind of unpretentious, affable demeanour that makes you want to be her friend. I leave the event smiling. I'm driven back to the city by an Uber driver who keeps repeating "Thank you, have a nice day, thank you," — in polite smiles until I'm out of the car.

An hour later, it's around lunchtime and I'm sitting down with my two project mentors. They take me to a cafe near the campus they teach in, which they frequent because it's "the only place around that speaks Italian." When a girl with a nose piercing brings out their regular order (two coffees and a plate of hot chips) my mentor responds, "Grazie," and the girl said the same. 

Karen and Andy had been my lecturers since my first year of uni. In 2018 they taught in one of my first lectures, and I still remember Karen's introduction ("We are partners in work and in life!"). They taught us about the history of design, from ancient Egyptian artefacts to the Bauhaus movement in 1920s Germany. We talk about future ideas, I ask them for advice, they ask me about my trip, I ask them about theirs too (to Italy soon).

They are artists in academia, but because they are artists — often with projects and commissions to finish by a certain deadline — they're the type of lecturers that say things like, "Try not to stress! We also have deadlines. So like you, we'll also be up at 2 AM, crying into our Red Bull." And then they'd laugh, and ask each other who's picking up the kids today. After we say goodbye, I continue my work in the library, feeling humbled that I get to learn from them in such close proximity.

Friday: My housemate and I are taking our cat to see the groomer. His fur had outgrown and every time we'd eat dinner, watching him, we say, "We need to take him to a groomer." We'd been saying this maybe for the past half a year, so it was about time. Plus my cat was starting to resemble a minuscule lion (but don't tell him I said that.)

That evening, I go to a friend's apartment for a potluck night themed A Taste of Home. We chat, and laugh, eating Taiwanese braised pork stew, Indonesian gado-gado, and Malaysian-style noodles. My friend had decked out her dining table in a way that felt as close to home as possible — a stone clay pot, coasters made of cork, Japanese-style Daiso dinnerware, a scented candle, mugs with personalities. We end the night with a board game, and she lets me borrow one of her suitcases. Again, I walk home that night by the Yarra riverside, smiling.

Sunday: A friend gives me a present, wrapped in brown paper with a floral sash and a bow. The envelope reads, "Dear Joanne (Happy birthday.)" Like me, she is also going away, except neither of us knows for how long. Unlike me, she has resigned from her job, and is leaving to start a clean slate. So unlike my other farewells, this one feels scarier, and less predicted — I don't know when I'll see her again. I save the present for my actual birthday, but I couldn't help it; I open the card that night. I read her handwriting, and by the time I reached her last sentence, I cried.

Tuesday: The weather oscillates between sunny and rain. It's just Melbourne being Melbourne, but it makes me think every sunny morning is the last one I get. So I treat it as such. It's my second-to-last day in the office, so I take my time walking to the train station, soaking every bit of sunlight.

The cafe I frequent is right across the train station. I do not know the owner's name, nor does he know mine, but this morning, he smiles at me in the finally familiar way. They're beginning to remember my order (three days before I leave, painfully). The owner hands me my coffee. As always, he says, "Have a nice day!" And as always, I smile back, "You too!" and rush out the door, crossing the street to catch my train.

Wednesday: I visit the same cafe, this time I sit down with some time to spare. I order a mocha and the girl serves me a latte, but I don't mind; I think she is new. My assumption proves true when the cafe owner — who's always behind the coffee machine churning out cups and cups of coffee for fellow morning commuters, dog walkers, and real estate agents on their way to a showing — started speaking to her.

"You don't have to be nervous, okay! Just be happy," I overheard him say. "Relax, chat with customers..." The girl smiles shyly, and says a few words in response, too quietly for me to eavesdrop.

​"Do you drink coffee, by the way?" cafe owner continues, "Would you like one? What would you like? I'll make one for you." This time I hear her answer, "Latte." "With sugar?" he asks. She nods, and on he proceeds to make her a cup.

I'm bad at getting cafe owners' names. There must be a category for shy conversationalists, and I'm right in the middle of it. I wish I've come here many times enough to have built this rapport (or the courage to just introduce myself), but alas, this will now have to wait until August: When the leaves are no longer yellowing, when there'll be no more dried crunchy ones littering the sidewalk. When there'll be no such need to put on so many layers, and maybe sunny days can stay for longer than two days. I imagine the train will run on its same timetable — the first one on time, and the second exactly 2 minutes late. I imagine the trees — how they'll all look a little more barren, but with new, sprouting leaves, budding in its place.
 
 

"​And so he told me his secret formula for happiness. Part one of the two-part plan was that I should just get on with ordinary life, living it day by day, like anyone else.

But then came part two of Dad's plan. He told me to live every day again almost exactly the same. The first time with all the tensions and worries that stop us noticing how sweet the world can be, but the second time noticing.

​And in the end I think I've learned the final lesson from my travels in time; and I've even gone one step further than my father did. The truth is I now don't travel back at all, not even for the day. I just try to live every day as if I've deliberately come back to this one day, to enjoy it, as if it was the full final day of my extraordinary, ordinary life."

​— About Time (2013)​
