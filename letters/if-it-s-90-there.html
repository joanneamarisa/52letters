If it's 90% there...

by Joanne Amarisa

If it's 90% there...

Melbourne, Australia. Monday, 4 April 2022.

My Dear Friend,

Remember how I wrote to you about dreams of flying elsewhere last week? Well, when my head isn't so up in the clouds — on the train ride to work, for instance — I watch the passing suburbs in the morning and actually feel quite lucky.

At 22, being able to say that I love my job feels like a luxury. There are many reasons, but one of them is I get to  pocket small nuggets of wisdom as I walk out of our Wednesday meetings. 

There are these bite-sized phrases my manager Suzanne likes to say, and I don't suspect she means to be profound, but I find myself repeating and pondering on every bit of advice. I plant them as seeds in my little mind garden and while digging the soil, I'm surprised by what I find. May I share with you some of them?


1. "You're so close to this."

Have you ever had a co-worker explain something in depth and then somewhere mid-sentence your brain just... lost the plot? Some days, our team would hold a meeting where Peter, our daily manager & office expert in analytics, would run us through a graph. I'm a sucker for charts, but frankly (and Peter knows this) there are moments where I (and the rest of the team) struggle to keep up.

It's like those lines in a TV show where the tech guy explains something and the protagonist goes, "... In English?" We find humour in these moments as Peter, bless his soul, can get carried away for a minute or two spewing terms that to him make perfect sense.

So in those moments, Suzanne would reach across the table and say:
 

"You're so close to this. We're not."


At which point Peter would backtrack and explain some of the gaps he might've forgotten to bridge.

I'd been on the other end, too. The other day I hadn't named my files properly (classic designer flaw) and Suzanne said the same thing. "Jo, you're so close to this, you probably know what that is. But we don't. So can you label them next time please?"

A term she likes to say, actually, is "you live & breathe this". It's true; you spend enough hours on something, be it a chart or a planner or a dashboard, and you start to work in your isolated knowledge of it.

But it's not just work; where else have I been so immersed in my own perspective that I simply forget to bridge the gaps for others?

I trace back to all the times I've pulled words, thoughts, presumptions out of my own pockets that to me, of course, feel so very right. I'm too close to it. I'm too close to whatever view I have of the world, reflected from my own inner world that doesn't get challenged very often. I'm too close to what I believe to be true – about people, about myself, or about current affairs – speaking in a vocabulary that pertains to these truths exclusively. In the spirit of practicing better empathy, perhaps it's time I say the word "obviously" less.
 

2. "If it's 90% there, it's done."

Progress over perfection, as the famous stock photo quote would say. Working in digital marketing trains me to throw out any and all ideas I have on 'perfect' — because it doesn't exist and it's a waste of time. 

It was hard at first: that logo can be more centred or we can try a different colour and I could write a better headline and there is always something swirling above my head that, if left unchecked, can lead me down a perfectionist's rabbit hole. But marketing is all about agility. You spend a minute more, and it costs you time and you risk losing to the competition.

Aiming for 90% doesn't mean we don't strive for the best output. We do, it's just that "done well & done now" is better than "done perfect but later".

So nowadays, I, too, am a big believer in 'beta-testing' everything. Don't know if I'm qualified... but why not just reach out? Don't know if I'm cut out for leadership... but I won't know until I try. Don't know if I could write a newsletter... but I'll send one out and see what happens. (And well, here we are!)

A 90% benchmark makes life so much easier to live. Not waiting for my work (or myself) to be 100% perfect makes the path clearer and less obstructed by self-imposed hurdles. Trading perfectionism for honesty is about letting the world see you a little undone, maybe even nervous, but showing up anyway. Allow life to be an iterative work in progress, leaving a trail of "final", "final-1", "final-2" drafts which are yes, about 90% there, but may lead us closer and closer to something we'll love.

(*Not to say that waiting for readiness has no merit — at least for most people I'd hope it applies to marriage or children — but even then, I see plenty of mothers start out clueless. So maybe we're still onto something?)

3. "You're one person."

If you're someone who thinks they can cram 4 tasks into the last 15 minutes of a workday, we might be very much alike. We're... What's a nicer word than over-achieving? Proactive to a fault?

This one came from a meeting where we were discussing our yearly planner. Looking at it, I suggested instinctively: "Oh. Well would you like me to do A and B and a little bit of C so we can get that campaign off the ground?" Something along those lines.

To which Suzanne looked at me and said, "Well, that would be great but we're going to need another you," she smiled. "You're one person."

It's a silly thing to need to be reminded of; that I am one person with only 2 hands, 2 eyes, 24 hours each day. Yet almost absent-mindedly I was ready to set myself the test of shouldering what would be a 2-to-3-person task.

Overestimating our own capacity can be a sign of equal parts people-pleasing and habitually putting ourselves secondary to the 'task'. This is probably going to burn me out, but I can handle it! 

Those in our 20s are so prone to letting work (or even 'passions') overtake the better of us, such as our health, our social lives, our rest. Even working overtime feels like second nature. And naturally, we're at the age of proving our own self-sufficiency. The less attractive truth, however, is we are just one person. Some might even say, self-sufficiency is overrated.

May I suggest: Asking for help — and learning to receive it? May I suggest honesty, to others and ourselves, about what's within our means and what probably isn't? Or may I suggest, at the very least — and I'm speaking this over myself, too — avoiding burnout not with a spa trip or a scented candle, but with not setting ourselves up voluntarily in situations that may lead to it in the first place?

Be a little kind to yourself — you're one capable, compassionate, trying-your-very-best person. :)
 


P.S. It's a gloomy morning here in Melbourne. When days are this grey and cloudy I like to play the soundtrack of my favourite movie. Enjoy the rest of your week :)
