11. What's your word of the year?

by Joanne Amarisa
Surabaya, Indonesia. Thursday, 23 June 2022.

My Dear Friend,

Every year for the past 2-3 years, I assign myself a word. They're not quite like New Year's Resolutions. (I am horrible at those, by the way.) These words are more like themes.

For 2019, it was bravery. I said yes to a lot of things, and was the most spontaneous I've ever been. For 2020, I faintly remember it as: I want to apologise less. I was someone who always felt apologetic for anything that involved setting boundaries or saying no, so that year I learned to say sorry for those things less. (Still learning!)

2022 ushered me in and I cannot remember how we reached the end of June so quickly. It felt like only yesterday we were celebrating New Year's Eve. As we approach the mid-year point, my word of the year has started to crystallise. After all its laughter, tears, and moments big or small, I decided my word for this year is audacity.

...

I like four-syllable words. They sound more powerful than I normally feel, and they lend my cadence a little more weight.

Oxford defines audacity as "A willingness to take bold risks." In the definition for audacious, Oxford adds: "A willingness to take surprisingly bold risks."

To me, audacity is finding the guts to challenge your long-held beliefs, go for what you want, and build a life you want to live without waiting for permission. Oxford is right — all of this is surprisingly risky. So here is how this risk-averse overthinker is practicing her audacity one day at a time.


In dreams

I awake in the aftermath of the pandemic and find dreams I'd nearly forgotten, scattered on my bedroom floor. That's right, I think, this is something I've always wanted. I pick it up, turn each one in my hand, and start my day with a reignited hope.

In the days of the pandemic, I don't know about you but when my brain (and sanity) kicked into survival mode, I very rarely thought about where I wanted to be the next 5-10 years. Understandably, we cycled day to day and focused on making it through the week. Out of the scary disease and the apocalyptic news cycles, this was a part of the pandemic that scared me the most — the threat to our habit of wishing.

Now that we've made it here, I reclaim a strength to consider that all our dreams are actually still possible. We are not behind, nor running late. I don't personally believe any year went by in vain, or in the concept of "lost time" because every season has something to teach me.

I hope that for you, this year can be about dusting off those cobwebs of doubt or even apathy. Whether it's pursuing studies, changing your career, or starting something new — this year, have the audacity to consider that if you are here and breathing, the life you want to lead carry at least the tiniest spark of possibility.

As Jesse Wallace mentions in the Before Sunrise (1995) trilogy: "You gotta be a little deluded to stay motivated."


In prayer

"I actually don't know what else to say," is something I say often when praying to God. Some days, I utter it when I'm swamped with gratitude, but other days, I refrain from speaking because I don't know if God will listen. I shy away from asking because I don't know if God will agree.

When I consider my thoughts so insignificant that I choose to hide them, I perhaps am forgetting that I am speaking to a God.

Practicing my audacity in prayer involves the audacity to 'waste His time'. To pray like my questions, desires, even messiest feelings matter to Him, because they do. Because everything about salvation and being a child of God is audacious. What Jesus did for me was undeservedly bold, and it has granted me the allowance to come to Him without any cost.

Most recently, I am practicing being comfortable in uttering wishful prayers: 

God, I’m gonna be audacious enough to ask You for this. I’m okay if Your design says otherwise. I just thought I’d ask. ❤️


In work

There are so many ways audacity can take form in my line of work, and many times I absolutely need it to.

To describe this, I am inspired by the tempo and mannerisms of my sister's 7-year-old son. When you scold a child, and they don't realise what they did was wrong, they put on their helmet of annoying curiosity: "Well why was I wrong? Why can't I do that? Why do you say so?"

I say helmet because nothing we say seems to get through to them — but I understand that to a child, these questions are genuine. They do want to know why they've been assigned a guilt they don't think they deserve, and in doing so, they are challenging our authority without even knowing it.

When you have spent years falling slave to your own inner self critic, audacity is the silent courage to finally start standing up for yourself. 

"You're not good enough."
 
Huh! Why do I feel not good enough?

"You're not cut out for this."
 
Really, though? Am I not? Then why am I doing it?

"You're not as good as she is."
 
Probably not. But I do know her, which means I can learn! Isn't it a great gift to be able to know and connect with people?

Each time I find the audacity to annoy my worst self critic, I challenge my own imposter syndrome and clear the path for my own growth. Audacity doesn't have to be this performative, pompous bravery you put on. Sometimes, it's as simple as speaking to yourself a little differently.


Choosing my word of the year doesn't mean I miraculously become it. Audacity is actually quite the opposite of how I feel on most days. I was raised in an Asian family, I'm the youngest daughter, and when I started out in my job a year ago, I remember feeling self-conscious about being the only woman of colour in the office. (In retrospect this was silly now that I know the incredible people I get to work with.)

But for most of us who are (more or less) on the same boat, the guts to speak up or go against the grain may not come so naturally. Thus my words of the year are not instructions, but compasses — helpful for times when I need to make tricky decisions, reassess relationships, or figure out my priorities — and as I grow older, these dizzying labyrinths find me more frequently.

It turns out that my yearly themes also follow a certain theme. In the past 3 years I've chosen Bravery, say sorry less, set boundaries, audacity. Between you and me, my Friend, I think I'm just trying to be more and more brave.
 
“The willingness to show up changes us. It makes us a little braver each time.”
— Brené Brown, Daring Greatly (2012)
