Autumn, and impermanence

by Joanne Amarisa
​Melbourne, Australia. Saturday, 30 April 2022.
 
"April prepares her green traffic light and the world thinks Go."
– Christopher Morley, John Mistletoe

My Dearest Friend,

Before I delve into this week's letter, I firstly wanted to say thank you. It is timely, that my 3rd letter, Out of hiding, was where this newsletter slowly peeked "out of its hiding" — I had invited a dear friend of mine to join our small readership, and when she asked, I allowed her to share the letter on her profile. Following that, we reached over 20 subscribers (nearing 30 now!), and for that I'm humbled and grateful. Thank you for allowing me to occupy a tiny space in your inbox. 

Last week I also wrote to you, fleetingly, about legacy and what it means to be outlived. The morning after, it reminded me of Disney Pixar's Soul, so I turned on its soundtrack made by the great Jon Batiste, and at the crack of dawn, listening to his laidback serenade, I put on my coat and walked outside.
 
...

Autumn has descended over Melbourne — and oh, I bask in its glory. Every tree is painted with tints of amber and at 7 AM, I'm greeted by a warm, glowing sunrise. It's not actually warm; it's about 8ºC. But marvelling at the golden light that lands on neighbouring apartments, speckled by shadows of branches and birds, warmth came from within.

Autumn reminds me of impermanence, as it always does. A season of transience; neither its beauty nor its cold agrees to stay with us forever. It arrives before we are ready, and leaves before we'd like it to end. Doesn't that sound familiar?

Seasons like these are time's way of breaking my ego, reminding me I have less power over the universe than I often think. When leaves are clinging onto a tree for dear life, and eventually fall, they remind me that even my biggest worries could someday vanish. My biggest, most recent mistakes, I may laugh about when I reach 30. Even the biggest longings in my heart — and as an early-20-something-year-old, they are many — are susceptible to change. Though it doesn't always feel that way, does it? My blunders keep me up at night for at least the following 5 business days, while my worries circle around me like schools of fish.

But the truth is this: Reckoning with impermanence is a practice in reducing our habit of wanting to outsmart what happens to us.
 
"Let everything happen to you: beauty and terror. Just keep going. No feeling is final."
– Rainer Maria Rilke

No feeling is final

There's no other word to describe it than humbling. When I feel victorious and the world is coated with shimmer, or when I am sad and my own shadow has grown twice its size, it is humbling to be told, Okay, this is how you are feeling now.

If I'm not careful, I might take offence or lose sight:

When ​I feel triumphant = What do you mean this isn't forever?

When I'm in grief = What do you mean this isn't forever?

It's a call to recognise feelings as existing, momentary things; not the be-all and end-all of really anything. How else would we know to never take our joys for granted? And isn't it the solace we need when we're in a devastating heartache, to know it won't always be as devastating as this​?

​
Embrace the now

As someone with the need to control and have things in place (wherever place is), being told that adulthood comes with unpredictable seasons that don't last forever is... tricky. "I want to plan out my life years ahead. What do you mean I'll know when I get there?" But that is often the only answer. 

Impermanence annoys me, but I succumb. Life's messy ebb and flow leaves me shell-shocked in its wake, but I play along.

Especially as an international, where life seems so nomadic. Having our existence in a certain place hinge on a travel document means we're always treading on borrowed time. This means when things are good, I cling onto them indulgently, "sucking the marrow out of life" as Thoreau would put it. Taking pictures, going on too many walks that cost me time, expressing my love for friends with fervent, almost-embarrassing affection...

And you know what? Almost always, even seasons that feel overplayed and cliché, have something to teach me. 


Let it help you

​Accepting our fleeting highs and lows means accepting the inevitability of changed priorities. This helps inform me on a couple of things. For example, distancing myself from people who would feel shocked or offended by this.

It also helps me avoid comparing my present self to past selves I no longer am, nor wish to become. For example just because I published more writing 4 years ago, doesn't necessarily mean I have to do the same now. (Because I don't want to do them now! I want to bask in my secret postcards.)

In performing my own personal life audit, impermanence helps me to ask: Is what I'm doing now in alignment with what I want to prioritise this month, this year? Am I surrounding myself with the people who are supportive of this?


Allow yourself compassion, and change

Contrary to popular belief, I sometimes think it's okay to "settle". (Okay hear me out). I'm not saying to settle for less than you deserve — my Friend, you deserve good things and you better believe it — but perhaps we often fall into the Social Media trap, or External Opinions trap, of where we "could be working", places we could be living, even the type of people we could be seeing.

Consider that it's okay if the good things you want and need, right now, aren't a shiny six-figure job, a two-storey house, or a Hallmark movie romance plot. (And if they are, how exciting and best of luck to you!) But Friend, you'll always find me celebrating your small wins — I took a walk! I left a toxic work environment! I said No to something I don't want! Because they are equally significant.

The run to chase anything under the sun is an all-consuming pursuit. Especially the chase towards "what we truly deserve". Whereas honestly? I think you are deserving of what you want and need at this point in your life. And if at any point that changes, I hope you are in the company of people who are compassionate of it.

As someone who has (1) turned down a dream job before, and (2) worked for 3 years in the wedding department, may I suggest to you this truth: That we all settle for what we need, and what we want, at a specific given time, and this applies to everything from the jobs we have to the person we marry. And it does not make those decisions any less valid or impactful, because each form the tapestry of this extraordinary ordinary life we lead and will look back on.

When we have this mindset that everything is final-and-forever, navigating adulthood may feel like stepping into a field of landmines. I now see it as it is: Just soil. So I sow, and sow, and sow.
 


To ponder: Even when things aren't always permanent, they are still worth the attention, joy, and hope. 

I pray the month of May treats you kindly.
