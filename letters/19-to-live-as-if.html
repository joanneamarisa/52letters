19. To live as if

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 1 September 2022.

My Dear Friend,

Happy spring. I know that's not entirely accurate to say given that you're all sitting in different corners of the world, but I couldn't resist the call for new beginnings.

One of the newsletters I subscribe to is written by a Product Designer named Budi Tanrim. In one of his letters last month, he wrote: "Next month, I'll quit my job."

He didn't quit – or at least, not that I'm aware of – but he reflected that this was how he thought about his job. It's derived from Bushido, a Japanese moral code for Samurai that means, "To keep death in mind." If a Samurai keeps death in mind and lived every day as if it were his last, it forces him to conduct his actions in the best way possible.

A life lesson from a death wish. Work like you're quitting. Budi asks us the question: "If you quit next month, do you feel you've made enough impact? Are you working on things that truly matter to you?"

Coincidentally, my mother turned on Netflix the other day to a movie called All My Life (starring Harry Shum, Jr.) I was sitting in the room, paying half attention when at the end, the monologue said:
 
"Everything can change in a day, in an instant. A full life can't be a series of forgotten days. If you're not happy, change. Shift gears. Take a chance. Later is not guaranteed."

I am not one to be impulsive – as much as I'd love to be the main character who makes rash, whimsical decisions that lead to the happiest moments of her life. On the contrary, I'm far more calculated, and far less brave. But I do agree: Later is not guaranteed.

So adopting the principles above, I reflect on my own life: If I were to leave Melbourne next month, what would I pocket with me? I won't pretend like I've never toyed with the idea (although that's a story for another letter.) I ask, What would I have done or gained, and more importantly, what would I have given? To this city, and to the people it has brought me?

Facing this question stresses me out. The same way reflecting on "what I have achieved / not achieved in my life" pushes me well near an existential crisis. But in the wake of the past two years – of being cooped up and having everything you love be so... disposable... I realise this is how my brain is choosing to cope. By registering how vulnerable we all are to change.

And so I need to live as if I'm not going to have the same chances tomorrow as I do today. What have I been putting off? If Heaven dragged me by the wrists and up for the skies, what would make me want to go back and say, "Not now!"?

I also realise, that to live "as if" is how we manage to survive.

In those two years of the pandemic, we were living as if things were going to get better. Despite every logic, every headline saying the opposite, this blind hope was what kept us going. In moments where I'm not seeing eye to eye with someone I love, I need to start seeing them as if I can lose them at any minute. Because — harsh truth — I can, and suddenly the questions no longer matter.

This perspective, albeit wildly extreme, enables me to see big picture, especially when I feel stuck going through the motions.

I do live "as if". I know I work a 9-to-5 in marketing which is hardly curing cancer or saving the whales, I know. And I know that people could be running businesses out of their homes or taking the same train commuting each day, and also feel the same fear: A fear of insignificance. 

But as the life-saving saying goes, "You gotta be delirious to stay motivated." I've gotta live as if it is the most important work I'll ever find myself doing. I'm not aiming for a significant life. I'm aiming for a life fully lived.

It helps to pull out your safety net of 'tomorrow' from time to time. I think each of us owes it to our dreams to meet them as soon as we are able to. Each of us has got to live as if what we are doing or creating or putting out into the world is life or death. Is now or never. Because otherwise, what reason do we have to keep going?
