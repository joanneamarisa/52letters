April 12, 2022

Out of hiding

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 13 April 2022.
My Dearest Friend,

When I am not at a good place — and the definition of this varies, from either "I've had a bad day" or "haven't exercised in more than 3 days" or "have a lot of grief and feel like no one in the world 'gets me'" — I feel like I want to hide.

Writing — or in general, showing up in the world as yourself — when you are 'not in a good place' is a hard thing. When I am not in a good place, a part of me says to the world, "No no no! Don't look at me!" Much alike the cries of Quasimodo, hiding in his bell tower.

I think we all have different hiding places. You may expect me to say something like a cigarette or a bottle, but there are far less nuanced, more convenient hiding places. Last Thursday, I was sitting in a pitch meeting led by a man, who must've been in his mid-40s. At the beginning of his pitch, he started using media jargon such as CRM or LPV or something. I forgot which topic it was, but before he moved on, he paused his pitch and said,
"Apologies if I use acronyms that make me feel important and special, but at any time, please feel free to stop me and ask."
It was refreshing. Like he'd peeled back a layer to say, Don't mind me. Just somewhat hiding. Because who doesn't feel important and special when sitting in a board room? With one of those long shiny meeting tables and maybe a fancy little laser pointer?

There I realised, rooms like these can be hiding places too. So can big, important words. Hiding places are what we run to when a part of us feels undone; often, it is a version of ourselves that we consider more 'acceptable'. For some, it's metaphors. For Quasimodo, it was his wooden miniatures. For others a book. An instrument. Or indeed, a cigarette and a bottle.

But if Quasimodo had never left his bell tower, how would he have realised that he was, in fact, lovable?

...

In about 5 weeks, I will be traveling home to see my family. Thoughts around hiding-vs-showing-up, I often equate to my feelings around returning to those dynamics. In a melting pot of giddiness and bubbling excitement, there are nerves and butterflies in my chest, which is silly to say about seeing the people who watched me grow up.

And yet. I lay awake at 1AM and think, Have I done enough? Would they consider me enough? 

These thoughts are (1) unnecessary, because I should know better than to seek others' approval for my self worth, and (2) uncharacteristic of the person I've become. I'm not one to criticise my life choices anymore, and in the past year I've grown to be big-sisterly-protective over said choices, and my own needs and boundaries.

And yet. I am wondering, like I used to when I was 16, about how I'd pass to parents and friends and parents of friends and friends of parents. Would I be proud? Would they be proud?

Why? My theory is that at the crux of discomfort, we search for somewhere to hide. We search for accolades or answers or costumes of selves that can make others or ourselves more comfortable. Discomfort can be internal: Avoidance of certain emotions or not wanting to answer necessary questions; or external: An important-and-special meeting room; a first, second date; making friends in a new environment. Understandably we hide when we evade being truly seen for who we are, which, if that's ever rejected, would be gravely more devastating.

But being seen is how we recognise that we are worthy of love and belonging, as we truly are. You're right, being seen also risks awkward conversations or people seeing you laugh a little too loud or people actually knowing you, at which point we cannot predict their acceptance. But could it be that our habit of hiding is why we stay in places not meant for us, for too long?

So how do we unlearn the instinct to hide? Well, dear friend, I'm in the midst of learning that (I have around 5 weeks left to learn it, to be exact), but here are a couple I can share with you:

I try my best to surround myself with people I never feel the need to hide from. I think it's important to be around people who make you feel like two things: Like you want to be the best version of yourself, and like you already are the best version of yourself, simultaneously.

They make you want to be better, not as a bid to alter yourself for their approval, but because they already see you, and believe in you, and that they will continue to see and love you, whoever you become.

(Bonus tip: I don't know about you, but I feel the most seen when I laugh. So maybe if you're trying to find those names, seek out the people in your life who can make you laugh.)

'Bad art' it out. This is more about addressing that discomfort — whatever you're dreading or anticipating or even confused about. Journal it out, but please, write badly. Yes, I forbid you to write a sonnet! If it's music, then don't even try to sing that well! Create as uncomfortably as how you feel and worry less about its outcome — this is a practice in showing up in the world as you are, less fixated on how you appear.

We are all one and the same, with our own vulnerabilities and Achilles heels and our own fears of the future and those handful of people we try hard to impress. So in sitting down with your discomfort, rest in the comfort that your troubles are less unique than you think.

Learning to neutrally sit with messier parts of our humanness helps us diffuse the intensity of these emotions, and give them a space to be held. And really all they need is to be held.

One of the most endearing things I remember about Quasimodo is when he finally emerged into the light, and joined the people of Paris as one of their own, kids loved him. They try and climb up his shoulders, and the townspeople knew his name.

It is out of hiding that we realise we have always been enough. I hope you swap the discomfort you don't let people see, for the discomfort of letting yourself be fully known, and therefore, fully loved. Because I promise, the latter helps you breathe so much easier. 
 
"Vulnerability is the core of shame and fear, and our struggle for worthiness, but it appears that it's also the birthplace of joy, of creativity, of belonging, of love." —Brené Brown
 

Tell me: When do you feel the most seen?

I wish you another wonderful, unpredictable week.
