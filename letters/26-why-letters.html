26. Why letters?

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Wednesday, 16 November 2022.

My Dearest Friend,

Oh, goodness. We are halfway through 52 Letters from Jo! How thrilling!

I found it exciting that this trail of postcards could capture a year of our lives. Who was I in the first letter I wrote? Who would I be in Letter 52?

This journey can only be bookended by two (potentially, and hopefully) different versions of us, if you think about it. One that's behind us and one that's a more mysterious amalgamation of ourselves, between now 'til however long it takes to get to Letter 52. Isn't that exciting and nerve-wracking at the same time?

Anyway, writing a letter to you each week has brought me great joy. Whoever you are, in whatever capacity you receive or enjoy them, I wanted to say thank you for being here.

I thought I'd mark this little milestone by sharing how this letter found you. What happened to the blog? some of you have asked. Why letters now? And why am I writing at all?

The blog

With Risa was a blog I wrote in from the years 2015 to 2021. Like this letter journey, it, too, was bookended by two very different versions of myself: 15-year-old Jo in high school who wanted to write about fashion — why did no one stop me?? — and 21-year-old Jo in her apartment during lockdown, completely terrified by an upended world but determined to finish university.

(Towards the end of it, she did :))

For 5-6 years the blog was like soil for writing practice, until I finally deactivated and brought the chapter to a close around the time I was turning 22.

One of my reasons for stopping was actually simple in that I started to feel an illusive pressure to perform. When every piece of craft is a performance, it's only a matter of time until we feel stage fright. I realised my fear had little to do with readership and more to do with my relation to writing that needed deeper addressing: What and who am I writing for?

So I decided, that even though I love reading other people's blogs (up to this day), I personally had to let go of what was no longer a healthy medium. 

The letters

And then the pandemic happened. During that time, I was inspired by one of my friends who posted a photo of a letter she got from her pen pal.

What? I remember asking. You have pen pals?? People still have pen pals??

Yes! she replied excitedly. She explained to me that her pen pal lived quite far from her. And here I was thinking it was a lost art!

And that's how I – spontaneously, but intentionally – asked 3-4 of my closest friends to see if they would like to be my pen pals. In the first few months of lockdown, it was their postcards and handwritten notes, both the receiving of and replying to them, that tethered my existence.

I think the magic that happens in letter-writing is it brings us back to the sacredness of expression. My one and only writing advice I ever give to people is to be vulnerable, and that's more possible to do when writing to a friend, as opposed to a sea of persons.

I liked the neatness of it, too – when I write my thoughts onto a piece of paper, sometimes paired with little doodles of the view from my window or a local bakery's pastry of the day, and send it away to my friend in an envelope, a part of me becomes safely theirs. Parts of them are safely mine.

One of my friends would mark hers with copious amounts of washi tape. Another would send me so many pages she had to number each side of paper. In isolation, we sought out connection, and found it in the simplest way of words. Not profound words, just honest words – of how we were feeling or what we were thinking of that day.

And it was in that one-bedroom apartment, penning letters to my friends, that I realised how much letter-writing encapsulates the reason I write at all.

The "why"

During and following that pen-pal phase was a year or so of me not really having an outlet for words. And without one I felt untethered. Even without the small legacy of a blog I was still typing Instagram captions on my Notes app at midnight, or scribbling poems into a journal no one would ever read.

Cliché as it may sound, I couldn't not write. And the reason why would be the same reason we so fervently seek out connection.

We can be rebellious or reserved or a mixture of both, but I think what each of us love is finding a constant in connection with each other.

I think we all feel bad about needing other people. Yet I think all of us love feeling needed. 

I think we say sorry for "saying too much" or "unloading too many stories". Yet I think all of us would jump at the opportunity to sit on a couch for hours, listening to our best friend's stories.

This, among many other things, doesn't make us incongruent or frauds. It's just one of the things that makes us human. When we open ourselves up to each other's experiences, we learn that the things we like or don't like about ourselves threads us together with others, more than it makes us unique. Because uniqueness can be a lonely experience.

When really: We all forget to water our plants and we've all said things we don’t mean. We all know the feeling of clammy hands and utter devastation and the thrilling euphoria of when we are mid-laughter. We all feel love and strain with parents or lovers. We all pair our fear of the future with a hope that tastes like delusion.

And beautifully, we reach out to each other as pen pals in semi-anonymity, stripped of our accolades and layers of self just to find solace in each other's words. In these letters, there is little interest in who we are out there in the world. In the fancy words we put next to our name on LinkedIn. Who are you now, hunched over your table? How did you feel today, who is on your mind and why? Leave your facades at the door. Come in and tell me all about it. 

I love letters because they verify to us that in every facet of our human experience, we are less alone than we believe.

All of this to say: I am very glad you're here. While I can't promise the next 26 letters to be profound - I will try my best to be sincere.

I may leave you with the advice that my 6-months-ago-self wrote, in her very first letter:
Go against the grain of what you've been telling yourself, and if you find it can make you feel excited about life again, wouldn't it be nice to see what happens then?
 
Always rooting for you from this side of the world. (And very interested in hearing your stories – about anything it might be, whenever you're ready to tell it:)

Your Friend,
Jo
