Hello! | Home & Starting Anew

by Joanne Amarisa

 
First and foremost, hello! This is my first time writing to you in this way, so I wanted to say welcome.
I'm afraid, my dear friend, that I'm writing to you today for utterly selfish reasons: I missed being able to write. So here I am, with not enough confidence to plaster my thoughts onto the Internet once more, but with enough silly little courage to slip a letter under your doorstep.
I can't tell you what these letters will be about. (Frankly, my new year's resolution was to 'try and see what happens'.) But I suspect I'll share with you stories about everyday moments that, to me, feel somewhat miraculous. I might write to you about triumph or grief, life and love, and other things you and I share that make us delightfully human.
In doing this, I will wonder how you've been, and hope my words offer themselves as, at the very least, a friend.
I send warm wishes your way. Please bear with me and sit tight.
With love as always,
Your Jo

My Dear Friend,

I am constantly barraged with thoughts of where I could flee at this very moment. This morning I stepped into a market to buy an iced coffee and thought, I'd love a ticket away, as I passed the aisles of fresh produce.

We're well into March which means it's a bit late to still be using terms like "this new year", but this new year, I have been addicted to starting anew.

In the span of a month between pre-Christmas to January, I had signed up for a dance class, pottery workshop, a 6-week barre intensive, and chopped my hair the shortest it's been. Smaller beginnings also ensued, such as buying new jeans or sipping my first espresso martini.

It's a whole "now or never" type of living now.

You may share the same feeling; a craving to jet plane elsewhere, try a new hobby, or do something refreshingly uncharacteristic for you to do, as we dust off the debris from our seen or unseen battles over the last 2 years.

What I'm suggesting may sound like escapism, and it felt that way at first. However as time went by, they began to feel like the opposite.

A couple weeks ago I walked into our dance studio and a woman sat next to me before class. Unpacking our things, I recognised her from the first time I joined — it had been her first time coming too. I introduced myself (after a long few minutes of mustering up the courage), at which point the woman, who came in with a quiet, sheepish smile, turned wide-eyed and excitedly introduced herself too. A minute later, we are raving about all the new shows in town — Hamilton, Moulin Rouge, An American in Paris — how she got tickets with her mother to see all of them, and for a moment, in an exchange of "I know right!" and "Oh my gosh same!" we were just revelling in being alive, and being there.

In remembering this I ponder, Is trying the uncomfortable a form of escape? Or is it a way of making myself even more at home?

Home is another debate: Whether it's in Melbourne, in my hometown, or in Jakarta, I frankly don't know. You may be reading this from Tasmania, Texas, New York City (and who among us know?) But just now, as I write this to you, I land on a verdict: Home is wherever we feel the freedom and courage to become.

My "try everything" conquest may sound like a manic quarter-life episode (not ruling that out yet). Escapist as it may sound, it's not to make me momentarily forget about life, but to solidify a sense of belonging in the life I already inhabit. I feel very at home with myself when I enter a class as an anonymous newcomer. Or when I leave a salon with freshly cut hair to the point of unrecognisable (yet I feel a little braver). I make conversation with the barista so I could love commuting to my 9–5. I stare at the ceiling of my friends' living room as we talk about our secrets, because when else will any of us be this young?

I've sat and walked and ran and stood in every nook and cranny Melbourne could offer me. Home is nowhere out here, but everywhere in the new memories that lullaby me to sleep. My friend's living room has been emptied and rented out. The stains of pottery clay on my shoes have disappeared. New adventures escape me as quick as they find me, you know? So I seek for new ones endlessly.

To wrap this first letter I may leave you with this: Break the shell if it means you can swim through life more comfortable. Go against the grain of what you've been telling yourself, and if you find it can make you feel excited about life again, wouldn't it be nice to see what happens then?
