44. On falling for my best friend

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Tuesday, 1 August 2023.

My Dear Friend,

"What we have is different," is probably a phrase that's been said or thought of by every couple in the world. So I won't say it. What you'll probably read about in this letter is a story as cliché as any other, and as unique as any other.

But it isn't about him—not exclusively, at least. I can give you a brief introduction, though, if you'd like.

Act I.

Serendipitous. The fact of finding valuable things by chance.

Ha. I wish it was as carefree as "chance", but in reality, it was something I contemplated for years, ruminated and prayed about for months, seeking counsel from three other people whom I trusted the most in this world, who bear a lot more wisdom than I. (You know me.)

So I did it. I took the leap. I fell, yet instead of crashing and burning, I found the softest landing.

I don't know what the ground rules are to posting about your significant other, or talking about them online. What do I say? What is useful for people to know? I can tell you he's funny, when he doesn't try. Isn't, when he does. He is kind, otherwise I wouldn't give him any of my time. He's of sound mind, not a mass murderer, and seems to maybe be a little smart, sometimes. (A hypothesis he proves and disproves many times.)

There's so much of him and us that I want to keep to myself. Trivial details and bigger milestone moments and facts that I imagine no one would have any use for.

But there are things I'd like you, dear Friend, to know about. 

Act II.

The cliché thing is that we started out as friends, and eventually fell for each other in tandem.

The less-cliché thing, is that I fell into love at a time where I imagined myself to be the furthest away from it I could possibly be.

At the start of this year, something tectonic happened in my life that left my heart tattered and heavily bruised. In writing this letter to you, it still is. I was in tears on the flight home to Melbourne last January, in the car that brought me home, in my bed for many nights, many mornings, throughout many phone calls, many walks.

The first time I said it out loud, was in a sunlit living room with my housemate at the time. It was a warm afternoon—the summer air in Melbourne had thickened within our narrow little apartment, the wall painted with leafy streaks of sunlight.

"My parents are separating," I said. And her jaw dropped, like mine did when I found out around Christmas time. I remember the silence that came after, as I let the story unravel, one word at a time. A few months later, I say it again to a different friend. And a few months after that, I say it once again.

With each recounting, it feels more and more like the truth that has sat there for ages. Like a reality that I'm finally starting to verbalise. Don't get me wrong: The heartbreak still spreads little fires everywhere. But the mental gymnastics of extinguishing each flame is something I've slowly trained myself to do over time.

To the point that it doesn't consume me. That though the grief is heavy, it no longer paralyses me. Though my rage is seething and palpable, I don't retaliate or resent. Sometimes the grief is a baby wrapped around my chest, toted with me as I go to work. Sometimes it's the elephant in the room. Sometimes the elephant is the room.

And it wasn't until a couple of people pointed it out, that I began to realise the unfathomable nature of my life this year: The little fires. The rock solid grief. The budding sprout of something new, amidst all this perilous terrain.

Act III.

How does one muster the hope, the audacity, to build a new relationship, while standing in the wake of the crash and burn of another?

It spun me in circles, dear Friend. Am I doing this in spite of the heartbreak? Because of it? Even though? And then I realised that it lends itself to no other than the simplest conjunction.

It has happened to me and. My heart has broken and.

My heart has broken and, it has also found for me something beautiful. My world's been torn and, it has also lit for me a new path when I least expected it. And, and, and. My worry and my hope. My heartache and my joyful surrender to being loved. The gratitude meets the grief. The two learn to co-exist, and not push either one away.

How did it happen? That part, I honestly have no idea of. My best wisdom (which isn't a lot) and my deepest courage (which isn't much) couldn't have planned the possibility of it. I do attribute it, though, to a Grace that couldn't have been mine. Mercy that couldn't have been from me. In the sliver of resilience that keeps our muscles pumping, I find we humans do regain the ability to love.

And that is what happened.

That in my own little world where I was carrying my elephant of grief, extinguishing my own fires everyday, someone stepped in, offered their hand, and suggested — in many attempts to my stubborn mind — that I don't have to go at it alone.

It was never my intention. If I had gotten a partner, I always wished I'd welcome them into a nice, clean terrain. Less flame-ridden than mine is, at least currently. But I've learned since that it's better to be loved as who we are, than as the facade we conjure up. And being loved and seen, through and through, no fires concealed, can give us the strength we didn't think we needed.

It helps if it's a person who can make you laugh. Who can also make you feel childlike, and normal. Who will gladly listen to your favourite playlist.

A poem that best describes our dynamic, his and mine, is my favourite by Sarah Kay, called Table Games. The part where she says, "Racing each other's brains to the cleverest quip / You were always faster than I / I could not keep up with your lightning mind and rejoiced, instead, in the satisfaction it gave you to beat me to the punchline. / To see you beam, at your own wit, and then at me, your favourite audience."

It's true; we banter and make fun of each other and take jabs at each other's faults (him at my dramatic-ness and me at his, well, everything), and that's how we know we see each other, and accept each other, in whole.

You deserve to be accepted, and seen, and loved, in whole. Though the path that led you here might not have looked crystal clear or perfect, you deserve to land somewhere you can be loved.

And I'm still not the type to think a partner's love triumphs over any other type of love. They are all one and the same once you think about it. They all require some level of trust, respect, appreciation, and a shared effort and commitment. They have all, in one way or another, made me choke up with tears of gratitude. They have all, in one way or another, left me in tears when things don't go my way.

I hope you choose to open up to the many paths that might not look like the one you've gone through before. That when love presents itself to you—in any type of way; a partner's hand, a best friend's hug, a shared table of pastries and coffee cups, a balmy summer afternoon—that you find the courage to let it in.

And I hope that after taking those first few steps, you can look back after all of it and see that your heart, though tattered and bruised, though worrisome or uncertain, is nonetheless held, and safe.

We all have our own little fires.
