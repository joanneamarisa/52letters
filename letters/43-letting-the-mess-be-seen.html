43. Letting the mess be seen

by Joanne Amarisa
Melbourne, Australia. Thursday, 6 July 2023.

My Dearest Friend,

I have a confession to make. My apartment is in a perpetual state of mess, and has been so for the past month at least.

For my 24th birthday, I asked my friends to buy me a dresser for my bedroom, knowing the apartment I'm in doesn't have enough built-in storage, and I have no furniture to compensate. So they picked for me a beautiful white-and-wooden 6-shelf dresser that will perfectly store all my clothes...

once it's assembled.

The reality is that the gift arrived in my doorstep shortly before my birthday in June, and then I flew to Singapore for a long weekend, chased to catch up with work, and was juggling 2-3 other things at once.

So what became of this beautiful dresser? Well, I did the work of unpacking the dresser from its box one day, and called it progress. I did a headcount of all the numbered panels of wood, metal rods, and little plastic bags filled with screws and littler parts, and called that progress. I organised them from 1-12—and yes, it's a 12-step manual—and left it there, so in other words: Not a lot of progress... For weeks I have lived with these random blocks of an unfinished furniture, strewn across the hallway that leads to my apartment.

Then a friend of mine suggested to come and spend the evening for a visit.

And as per the Disney Pixar movie Inside Out when chaos erupted in the cockpit, red sirens and all, all my little emotions went to sheer panic mode: Ahhhh no no no no, I'm not ready I'm not ready I'm not ready!!!

(My tall, lanky Fear, most of all.)

But as I sat with it, I pondered again on how I was feeling. Because up until then I had only planned to invite them once—and only once—I had finished this Goliath-task dresser. 

And I asked myself again: Why? I felt adequately ready for them to see my apartment; in fact, I've had friends see my apartment before. Sure, it could always use another vacuum, the coffee table and my work desk could always be neater, and sure, I have yet to learn the concept of a "tidy" rack to place my shoes, and the plate from which I ate my last meal might still sit in the bottom of my sink.

But it is as much of a home as any. I myself have stepped into some messy apartments of my friends' before. Like lone-sock-on-the-living-room-floor, tower-of-dishes-that-hits-the-sink-faucet messy—and no! Of course these did not scare me off from our friendship, ick me to the point that I had to leave, or even make me think of my friends any less. Friendships well withstand messy homes!

So why was it that I was there, across from a friend who was as open and understanding and close to me as anybody, feeling an insane amount of embarrassment for this dresser that had not been finished? Ashamed over a cardboard box and a few panels of wood that sit against the wall of my hallway? What had repulsed me about the idea of somebody coming into my home before it was perfect? Simply because my home was, at that given time, a work in progress?

This leads me to a deeper question about a tendency I didn't know I had, until a couple of my close friends had pointed it out to me in the past:

"You know, Jo, every time you share something to us... it's always really... polished. It's not a bad thing, it's good! But also... you know you're allowed to vent, right?"

I remember being flabbergasted by this comment.

Because wouldn't that be... irresponsible? I thought. "I vent to you all the time!" I retaliated. "I feel like I even share too much already, to the point of becoming a burden."

And my friends quickly shook their heads, "Not at all! When you share, sometimes it feels like your problems are neatly wrapped. It already has a story, resolved with a beginning to end. But, you don't have to be so put together and polished. You can be open, and just pour it out."

You can be open, and just pour it out.

So, Friend, I suppose that deeper question I was referring to was this: Where did we learn that we need to pull ourselves together, appear or become perfect, before we can let anyone in?

Going back to present time, I soon found it comical—that my genuine plan of action earlier last month was to single-handedly build this dresser myself (even though the instruction manual says it needs a 2-person assembly!), and only then will I begin inviting my friends into my apartment again. You know, once I've finished it, and all the cardboard is thrown out, and I have changed over my rugs, and I have upgraded my vacuum to give it a deeper clean, and I have wiped every surface of any speck of dust the eye can see...

How exhausting! It would've been close to my 25th birthday before I'd invite anybody in, it sounds like.

Where did I learn to never let anybody see the "chaos"? Whether it be a messy apartment, a complex set of emotions, thoughts in my head I have yet to untangle?

Some of us grow up with this armour-like mechanism. Because of course, vulnerability is the least preferred, and more difficult, route. Pouring your heart out puts you at greater risk of "the wrong reaction", the way I felt that my messy apartment would put me at risk of judgment.

As a result, something I unearthed in a recent therapy session was that I invite people into difficult times (even my most trusted people) only when I am ¾ of the way through a problem. If the average difficulty I had in my life was a hill, it would be once I get past the peak that I share what I'm going through with somebody else. 


Sometimes, I even only confide in them when I'm all the way through, and the hill is well behind me. Through therapy, I learned this habit can be exhausting. Because doesn't that mean I'm trudging up that hill, all by myself, all the time? Doesn't that mean I'm insistent on living out the more challenging, the more uncertain, the messier aspects of my human experience, all on my own?

My therapist said, "My advice to you, Jo, would be to experiment showing up unpolished."

I want to practice not having to put on a perfect facade before letting people in. 

Because this unfinished dresser is much like the many other Goliath-sized tasks we may encounter: navigating changes, tough relationships and conversations, and any form of complicatedness we may find ourselves in as we become adults. Even the Goliath-sized task of taking care of ourselves, sometimes! Especially when life gets tough and our mood or state of mind suggests anything but.

The way my therapist described it is: Taking care of ourselves is a Swiss army knife. Self-regulating and figuring it out on your own, is one blade in a set of blades. At times it is helpful! But there are other tools to use. Opening up, being vulnerable, asking for help, are equally helpful tools. And most of the time, they are tools that you will also have at your disposal. Using only one blade from your whole toolkit for every single task would be ineffective, and yes, of course tiresome.

So I went home that evening, and texted my friend.

"Are you free to come over to my apartment to help me build this dresser?" I asked.

"Of course!" she said in response.
