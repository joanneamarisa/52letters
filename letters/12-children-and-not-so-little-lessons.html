12. Children and not-so-little lessons

by Joanne Amarisa
Surabaya, Indonesia. Wednesday, 29 June 2022.

My Dear Friend,

It must've been when I found myself sitting on my bedroom floor, playing a game of Monopoly with a 7-year-old boy, that I finally thought: 

"What.... am I doing...."

I swear months ago, I would've logged off work and simply minded my own business — maybe even go for a shop or meet a friend for a cocktail. Fast forward to June and I am hunched over a 4-year-old's fingernails, putting on glitter nail polish and struggling for dear life to get them on her tiny, tiny fingertips, in a colour that, she says, would "go with her outfit tomorrow."

Oh, I'm forgetting context: They are my sister's children. Seven, four, and one. My sister, a hands-on stay-at-home mother who swears against hiring babysitters, shares a house with us. Thus, for the past 6 weeks I've been introduced to a delightful, chaotic brand new experience: Living with children.

I'd love to tell you about it, if you'd let me.

First, the clear marking of territory. There is a part of the house that my parents inhabit; marked by bookshelves and memorabilia and sensible office supplies — so adult and rectangular. And then, there's the rest of the house, identifiable by half-eaten cereal bowls, drawing books, Barbie dolls, and Play Doh... So, so much Play Doh... I'm sure you can guess who runs this part.

Second, is the somehow omnipresence of little children. They're somehow just... everywhere. All the time. 

Third, is the noise. It's always when I'm sitting at our breakfast table at 7 AM, peacefully admiring my mother's backyard — sipping my tea, reading my book when all of a sudden, it's 7:15.

"Auntie!!!!"

I haven't finished my book in weeks.

And yet. Even though it's true that the house is rarely silent, that a mess is always imminent and my personal space is loosening as a concept, I strangely adore co-existing with the little gremlins. Hearing their footsteps is how I know the day has officially started, and their laughter returns music to this home. 

We're never short of daily shenanigans that drive us crazy, but even then, my sister takes care of it like the supermom she is. “I’m not leaving my kids to strangers,” she explained to me. "I'd rather bear the tiredness of raising them ourselves." I'm the little sister who gazes in admiration. But of course, then I too would have my fair share of babysitting single-handedly.

Hence, we circle back to my bedroom floor. It is 10 PM. The Monopoly game isn't finished. I have work the next morning and couldn't sleep yet because... well... because the 7-year-old says so.

In moments like these, I'm reflecting constantly. Not about what plot of land to buy, mind you, but about what to say to them. About whether any of these minutes we spend together will somehow form into core memories — the way I have core childhood memories of unremarkable occurrences with the people closest to me.

And here is what I discover — what I subtly try to teach them and what, in return, they teach me.
 

1. Teaching kids about the world

My nephew — the 7-year-old sitting across the Monopoly board from me — is a bit of a trivia wiz. (Or a know-it-all in the making, we haven’t quite decided yet.)

When we’re together, he asks me things like: What is a cruise ship? What do monks do? Did you know what happened to the 16th US President? (Between you and me, I'm just wiping my brow each time I happen to know the answer.)

The kid thinks in facts. For example, he knows a plane had crashed into the Twin Towers in 2001. But because he's a child, he doesn't know about the abstract concepts that come with the facts — like what actually caused the plane to crash and why, because that would mean a concept of 'there is bad in the world but not always'. Even more mind-twisting was when the boy recited to me the shootings of Abraham Lincoln and JFK, with an unnerving amount of detail. "His mom called him John, Auntie," he told me, "but other people called him Kennedy."

I'm a bit shaken by the fact that Google has served up these stories to this sweet 7-year-old boy so easily. I don't know how his brain is making sense of it. The boy is far smarter than I am, so I don't waste my time trying to appear adult or all-knowing. So I figure it's just about helping him understand.

So whenever possible, I invite him to dip his toes into thinking critically with me. Disclaimer: He doesn't always want to join me. But when he does, I take the chance to start a bigger-picture dialogue that sounds a bit like:

"That's not okay, right? When people hurt people? What happens when we hurt other people?"

Again, I'm just winging it, but if I'm lucky, he'd respond with a "Well, that's sad" or "Yeah it's wrong."

Raising big questions to a child teaches me that we bridge when we simplify. In a similar vein, raising dialogues with people who hold vastly different ideologies or understanding from me is not about whose fact gets the last word, but about who's willing to try to understand. 

I try to project 5-10 years into the future and hope that the boy raises the same questions when his classmate comes to him with facts. Or at least draw his own conclusions about the good and bad of the world and what to make of it. (Isn't that what we're all constantly doing anyway?)
  

2. Teaching kids about actions

At the dining table, I look at the way my sister and her partner educate their children on what is and isn't acceptable. I notice they are less concerned with 'order'.

"When the girls turn 17 and ask their dad if they can go to a night club," my sister joked, "he's probably gonna say, 'Sure.'"

But they are firm when it comes to respect. I learn that this goes far beyond “What do you say when you receive something?” Reminding kids to say sorry, please, and thank you is just basic programming of social cues. Having to model the actions, behaviour, and compassion you expect from them yourself — that's a whole thing entirely.

For example, my sister limits their screen time, so accordingly, I don't give these kids my phone even when they reach for it. That's a rule. But that also means I can’t constantly be seen checking my phone when I’m spending time with them. That's a standard, set through modelled behaviour.

Even as adults, how many times do we place standards on other people that we're less willing to live up to ourselves? Isn't it still the case that the parts we dislike in others are parts we saw in ourselves first?
 

3. Teaching kids about consent

One thing I learn from my sister is she doesn’t force her child to hug anyone they don’t want. We were chatting about this, and we've both seen some parents really push their kids to do the opposite. "Hug / kiss your relatives before you go — otherwise you're impolite." My sister is far less strict, but I think it's because she's far more protective.

Following her cues, I try to apply the same — I make a habit of asking the kids, “Do you want to give Auntie a hug?” rather than assuming they want to give me one. Again, this is often a moot point, because they are children, and our personal space to them is already non-existent. Still, my hope is it trains the muscle in their thought pattern to realise they do have a choice. They always have a choice.

Especially today, I am trying to advocate (in micro scales like these) about not having to physically interact with anyone in any way you're not comfortable with. This escalates anywhere from behaviour-modelling for my sister's children to the phone call I have with my friend when she tells me about the guy she recently started dating.

At the end of each night, the kids do knock on my door. We hug good night, wave good bye, and as they slowly close my door, I only ponder that out of all the choices in the world, I'm lucky to be one of theirs.
